What is Loom?

Loom is a 2D animation and generative art environment. It covers a broad range — from purely algorithmic, computationally-driven work at one end to traditional cel-style character animation at the other — and most productions sit somewhere between those poles, combining generative elements with conventionally animated sprites or imported artwork.

At the generative end, Loom's recursive subdivision engine and parametric driver system let the machine generate structural complexity from simple geometry: one polygon becomes thousands of sub-polygons; a noise driver on a renderer colour produces organic variation across an entire frame range; jitter pools create rapid, unpredictable style switching frame by frame. At the animation end, SpriteCycles sequence geometry and bitmap frames with per-state timing and transitions; sprite hierarchies with inheritance masks support rigged characters; keyframe drivers handle position, rotation, scale, opacity, morph targets, and active set assignments with the precision expected of a dedicated animation tool.

Four affordances are central to how Loom works across both modes:

  • Separation of concerns. Geometry, subdivision rules, and rendering style are defined independently and combined at render time. The same polygon can be subdivided twenty different ways and rendered twenty different ways — none of those choices are baked into the geometry itself. Changing a subdivision rule or renderer propagates instantly to every sprite that uses it.
  • Recursive subdivision. A single polygon can be subdivided repeatedly, each pass splitting its children according to a rule. Five levels of Quad subdivision on a square produces thousands of sub-polygons. The emergent complexity arises purely from the algorithm and the initial geometry — you draw one shape, the machine multiplies it.
  • Parametric animation. Properties do not only move between keyframes. Noise, oscillators, jitter, and set-swap drivers produce behaviour over time without any keyframes at all. These compose naturally with conventional keyframe animation on the same sprite.
  • Cycle and image animation. SpriteCycles step through geometry states and image frames with independent hold and transition timing, enabling walk cycles, expression sequences, and mixed polygon/bitmap animation on a single sprite. Image sprites — SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, GIF — are first-class citizens alongside polygon geometry, with all standard transform drivers and camera features applying equally to both.
A note on approachability. Loom's full pipeline — geometry → subdivision → rendering → animation — requires understanding each stage before they combine effectively. For generative work that rewards this investment. For traditional animation you can skip subdivision entirely: place image sprites or assign simple geometry, rig a hierarchy, and drive the timeline — no subdivision knowledge required. The Foundations section introduces the core geometric concepts if you are coming to Loom without a background in vector geometry or subdivision.

Core workflow

The generative pipeline and the image/cycle animation path share the same sprite and timeline infrastructure — only the middle stages differ.

  1. Create or open a project. The project folder holds geometry files, brushes, stamps, image sprites, and project_config.json.
  2. Geometry path: In the Geometry tab, draw or import polygon, open-curve, and point geometry. In the Subdivision tab, define parameter sets and assign them to sprites. In the Rendering tab, create renderer sets and assign them to sprites. Image path: Place SVG, PNG, or other image files in svgs/sprites/ and assign them directly to sprites in the inspector — no subdivision or renderer set required.
  3. In the Sprites tab, create sprite instances. Set position, scale, and rotation. Establish parent–child hierarchies for rigged setups. Use the Scenes panel at the bottom of the Sprites tab to create multiple scenes if the project needs several distinct animations sharing the same assets.
  4. In the Layers tab, organise sprite sets into compositing layers — each with its own parallax factor, blur radius, opacity, and blend mode. A background layer at parallax 0 stays fixed while the camera pans; a foreground layer at parallax 1.2 moves faster, creating depth. Blur simulates depth-of-field. If you need no depth separation, leave layers empty — all sprites render in a single flat pass by default.
  5. In the Cycles tab, define SpriteCycles for walk cycles, expression sequences, or any multi-state animation. Assign cycles to sprites in the Sprites inspector.
  6. Add animation via the Sprites inspector or the Timeline. Keyframe drivers handle transforms, morph, opacity, active subdivision set, renderer set, and active cycle. Procedural drivers (noise, oscillator, jitter) are available on any numeric lane.
  7. Press Play (Space) to preview. Export as a still, SVG, or video from the toolbar.

Foundations: Geometry, Curves & Subdivision

This section is a gentle introduction to the geometric ideas that Loom is built on. If you already have a background in vector graphics or computational geometry, most of this will be familiar and you can skip ahead. If you are coming to Loom primarily as an artist, read through once — the concepts here explain why Loom behaves the way it does and will help you make better creative decisions with it.

Vector geometry

Points and coordinates

Everything in Loom starts with points — positions in 2D space described by two numbers, an x (horizontal) coordinate and a y (vertical) coordinate. A point at (0, 0) is the centre of the canvas. Positive x goes right; positive y goes up.

Unlike raster (pixel) images, which store a colour for every pixel in a fixed grid, vector geometry stores only the mathematical descriptions of shapes — the coordinates of corners and control points. This means a vector shape can be drawn at any size without losing quality, and it can be precisely manipulated, subdivided, and transformed by algorithms.

Polygons

A polygon is a closed shape defined by an ordered list of points (called vertices) connected by straight edges. A triangle has three vertices; a square has four; a complex silhouette might have hundreds. The edges are implied by the order of the vertices — each vertex connects to the next, and the last connects back to the first.

Loom works primarily with polygons. Every piece of geometry you draw or import is stored as one or more polygons. Subdivision takes those polygons and splits them into smaller polygons according to a rule — but every result is still just polygons, which is why the process can be repeated recursively without limit.

Open curves and point clouds

Loom also supports open curves (a sequence of vertices that does not close) and point clouds (isolated dots). Open curves are subdivided and rendered as strokes rather than filled shapes. Point clouds are rendered as individual dots or brush stamps placed at each point position.

Why keep geometry separate from style?

In most drawing software, the stroke colour, fill, and line weight are properties of the shape itself. In Loom they are not — the polygon stores only the vertex positions. Colour, fill, brush, and rendering mode are all defined in the renderer set, which is assigned to a sprite at the project level. This separation means you can take one polygon and instantly compare it with twenty different rendering approaches, or share the same renderer across twenty different geometries. It is a deliberately computational model that enables systematic visual exploration.

If you want visual material where geometry and style are already combined — an Inkscape illustration, a painted character, a photograph — Loom accommodates that directly. Place an SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, or GIF in the project's svgs/sprites/ folder and assign it to a sprite as an image sprite. The file renders exactly as authored, with its own embedded colour, shading, and texture intact. All of Loom's transform drivers, camera effects, parallax, hierarchy, and compositing features apply to it just as they do to polygon sprites. The separation-of-concerns model is an option, not a constraint.

Bézier curves

What is a Bézier curve?

A Bézier curve is a smooth curve defined by a small number of control points. The curve passes through the first and last control points (called anchors) and is pulled toward — but does not necessarily pass through — the intermediate control points (called handles). The mathematical formula blends between the control points using a parameter t that runs from 0 to 1, tracing the curve from start to end.

Loom uses cubic Bézier curves — four control points per segment: two anchors and two handles. Every edge of a polygon in Loom can optionally carry a curve weight and direction that bends it as if it were a Bézier segment. An edge with zero curvature is a straight line; as curvature increases the edge bows inward or outward.

Bézier curves in Loom

You can control edge curvature directly in the Geometry editor using the Edge Curvature tool. The curvature value and direction are stored per-edge in the geometry file. Subdivision algorithms are aware of curvature — the Bézier Midpoint setting in the Subdivision tab places subdivision points along the Bézier arc rather than on the straight line between vertices, so curved polygons subdivide smoothly rather than collapsing to angular shapes.

Curve data is also preserved when importing SVG paths — Loom converts SVG cubic Bézier segments into its internal edge-curvature format automatically.

Recursive subdivision

What is subdivision?

Subdivision is the process of splitting a polygon into smaller polygons according to a rule. The simplest rule is Quad: find the centre of the polygon and connect it to the midpoint of each edge, dividing the polygon into four children (for a quadrilateral). Apply the rule again to each child — now you have sixteen polygons. Apply it a third time — sixty-four. This exponential growth from a single starting shape is what makes subdivision such a powerful generative tool.

Why recursion matters

The power of recursive subdivision is that complexity emerges from simple rules. You do not draw the 64 child polygons — you describe a rule and an initial shape and the machine generates the rest. More importantly, every child polygon is still available as a first-class object for further manipulation: each child carries its own position, can be assigned its own renderer, and can itself be further subdivided using a different rule at the next level.

In Loom, subdivision parameters sets define chains of rules. A set might say: apply Quad at level 3, then apply Split at level 1 to each child, then apply Echo at level 2 to each of those children. The result is a three-step recursive refinement of any polygon fed into it. Changing a single ratio parameter in the Quad step immediately regenerates every polygon downstream in real time.

Subdivision and animation

Because subdivision is recomputed every frame, its parameters can be animated like anything else. Animating the ratio or inset of a Quad step — the position of the subdivision cut — causes the child polygons to visibly slide and breathe within their parents. Animating the PTW or PTP transform (which shifts child polygons inward, outward, or rotated within their parent cell) creates more dramatic structural motion. No extra geometry is required — the animation is entirely in the parameters.

Start simple. A useful first exercise: draw a rectangle in the Geometry editor, create a sprite that uses it, add a Quad subdivision set at level 3, and try different ratios (0.3, 0.5, 0.7) and inset values. Then switch the algorithm to Tri or Star and observe how the child structure changes completely from the same starting polygon. This is the core loop of working in Loom.

Quick Start

New project

  1. Choose File → New Project… (or ⌘N). Enter a name in the dialog and click Create.
  2. Loom creates the project folder and opens project_config.json with sensible defaults (800×800 canvas, 30 fps).
  3. The window switches to the main layout — the five tab panels and playback toolbar become active.

Open an existing project

  1. Choose File → Open Project… (or ⌘O) and select the project folder, or click the Open Loom Projects Folder icon in the toolbar and double-click a project.
  2. Loom reads project_config.json and reloads all geometry, renderers, and animation state.
  3. Use ⌘R or the reload button to re-read the config after making external edits.

Save As

Choose File → Save As… (⌘⇧S) to duplicate the current project under a new name and location. Loom flushes any pending saves, copies the entire project folder to the chosen path, updates the project name in the config, and switches to the new copy. The original project remains untouched on disk. Use Save As to create a branch of a project (e.g. "MyFilm_v2") without losing the original.

Evolution Seed

At the bottom of the Global tab's inspector, below Status and Utility, a live readout shows the seed currently driving whichever Generational Evolution pass is selected in the Transform tab — updating every frame as the timeline advances. With that pass's Vary seed per cycle off, this just mirrors its Seed field. With it on, the value changes each time the reveal driver completes a cycle, since a different effective seed is in play each time round.

This exists so you can watch an animated reveal, spot a generation you particularly like, and reproduce it exactly: select the text next to Seed here, paste it into the pass's own Seed field (in the Transform tab's Evolution inspector), and turn off Vary seed per cycle. The pass now always regrows that specific shape rather than continuing to vary.

Nothing shows here unless a Generational Evolution pass is selected in the Transform tab — momentum drift and convergence pressure passes, or no selection at all, leave this section showing a placeholder instead.

Quick setup

Quick setup creates a working pipeline from a geometry source without requiring separate dialogs for every hidden shape and shape-set object. Shape sets and shapes still exist internally, but the user-facing workflow concentrates on geometry, subdivision, sprites, render sets, and renderers.

  • Suggested names are generated automatically from the geometry name. All name fields are editable — type directly to override the suggestion, or click the ⌄ chevron beside each field to pick from items that already exist in the project.
  • If the geometry is not subdividable the subdivision field shows None. Polygonal geometry can also be deliberately assigned None when subdivision is not wanted.
  • Existing renderers are preserved. When the renderer set and renderer name you enter already exist in the project, a green badge reads Existing renderer — definition unchanged and the Mode picker is hidden. Make Pipeline wires the sprite to that renderer without touching its colours, stroke width, or any other setting. This lets you share a carefully designed renderer across multiple sprites without accidental resets.
  • Make Pipeline creates the necessary behind-the-scenes links but leaves you in the current tab. A visual status badge confirms whether the pipeline exists.

Extending a freehand open curve with P2P

A common cel-animation task is to draw an organic foreground outline with Freehand — a landscape edge, a floor line — and then neatly close it to form a filled polygon by connecting the two endpoints via straight-line corners. Doing the whole shape in freehand is tedious; doing it entirely in P2P loses the organic quality. The extension workflow combines both:

  1. Draw the organic outline using the Freehand tool. Release without closing the stroke so it becomes an open curve. The open curve is automatically selected.
  2. Switch to Point-by-point. The open curve selection is kept — you are now in extension mode.
  3. Click each corner or base point on the canvas. An orange ring marks the curve's end anchor so you know where the chain is anchored; the draft line continues visually from there. A dashed closing line shows the path back to the curve's start anchor.
  4. Press P (or click Finalise polygon) when all corner points are placed. Loom merges the open curve's cubic segments with straight-line P2P segments, adds a straight closing segment back to the curve's start, and produces a single closed polygon. The open curve is removed.
Example — foreground landscape for a layered scene: Draw the top silhouette of a hillside with Freehand (open curve). Switch to P2P and click the bottom-right corner of the canvas frame, then the bottom-left corner. Press P. The result is a closed polygon whose top edge is the organic hill outline and whose bottom three edges are the straight frame border. Import it into the sprite's geometry, set the layer to the foreground depth, and use a fill renderer.
  • Extension mode requires exactly one open curve selected and at least one P2P draft point.
  • The freehand portion retains full cubic Bézier data; P2P segments are straight-line cubics (inferred smooth handles).
  • The resulting polygon is named after the original open curve unless it was an auto-generated "Freehand Curve N", in which case it is renamed "Polygon N".
  • If you press Finalise polygon with an open curve selected but no draft points, the curve is closed with a single straight segment from end to start — the standard quick-close path.
  • Undo covers the whole operation as one snapshot.

With either knife tool active, Shift-click anywhere on an existing polygon or open-curve edge to insert a new anchor point at exactly that position.   • Works on both polygons and open curves.

  • Full undo support — press ⌘Z to remove the inserted point.

Using the Curved Knife

  1. Select the Curved Knife tool from the right panel (the dashed-curve icon beside the straight knife).
  2. Drag across the geometry to define the cut chord. An orange dashed bezier curve is drawn; initially it is straight (control points sit at ⅓ and ⅔ of the chord).
  3. Release the mouse. The tool enters adjust mode — two open-circle handles appear on the arms extending from each endpoint.
  4. Drag either handle to bend the curve. The orange dashed line updates live. Each handle moves independently.
  5. Press K or Return to commit the cut. Geometry that the curve crosses is split at the intersection points, exactly as with the straight knife.
  6. Press Escape, or start a new drag, to cancel and discard the current curve.

Anchor-Point Knife Cuts

Both the straight knife and the curved knife have an anchor-point mode that defines the knife line by Command-clicking two existing vertices rather than dragging. The two points can be on the same polygon or on different polygons in the same layer — the behaviour differs in a useful way.

How to use it
  1. Activate the Knife or Curved Knife tool.
  2. Command-click on an existing anchor point. An orange filled circle marks the first endpoint.
  3. Command-click a second anchor point in the same layer. An orange dashed preview appears between the two points.
    • Straight knife: the preview is a straight dashed chord.
    • Curved knife: the preview is a dashed bezier curve with two open-circle control handles. Drag either handle to adjust the curvature before committing.
  4. Press K or Return to commit the cut.
  5. Press Escape at any time to cancel and discard the selection.
Same polygon — topological split (no new points)

When both anchors are on the same polygon, the polygon is split exactly at those two vertices. No new anchor points are created — the selected vertices become the shared corners of the two resulting polygons. This is the cleanest possible cut for cases where the desired split points already exist in the geometry.

Different polygons — cross-boundary cut

When the two anchors are on different polygons in the same layer, the line between them acts like a regular knife drag across all geometry in that layer. New intersection points are inserted wherever the line crosses a polygon edge — including any shared edge created by a previous cut. The anchor positions lock the knife's start and end exactly to those vertices.

Example: after splitting a fish shape horizontally from mouth to tail, Command-click an anchor on the top half and an anchor on the bottom half to make a vertical cut. The horizontal middle edge is intersected automatically, producing a new point on it, and both halves are split cleanly.

The status bar shows "will intersect intermediate geometry" to confirm you are in the cross-polygon path.

Notes
  • Both selected points must be in the same geometry layer. Clicking a point on a different layer is ignored.
  • Clicking the same vertex twice is ignored.
  • Starting a drag (without Command) while anchor-point mode is active cancels the anchor selection and starts a normal knife drag.
  • The all-visible-layers scope toggle does not apply to anchor-point cuts — the operation always affects only the layer that owns the selected anchors.

Extrusion and rendering

The two extrusion tools (Displacement extrude and Scale extrude) build three-dimensional box-like structures by stitching new quad faces between the source geometry and a displaced or scaled copy. Each face of the resulting solid — the top surface, the front panel, the side panels — is stored as a separate polygon in the geometry layer.

This multi-face structure has an important rendering implication. When a renderer such as Basic fill processes the layer, it fills each polygon independently. Where two faces share an edge, their fill paths meet but are composited separately: sub-pixel anti-aliasing from each polygon bleeds across the boundary from both sides, producing a faint seam line at every internal edge. Diagonal triangulation seams are also visible where the renderer has split a quad face into two triangles. These artefacts are intrinsic to the geometry — they are not a renderer setting and cannot be eliminated by adjusting fill parameters.

Why the same fill looks clean on a P2P shape. A polygon drawn with the Point-by-Point tool is a single closed path. One fill operation covers the whole shape with no internal boundaries, so there are no compositing seams to reveal.
Using extrusion as a construction scaffold

Extrusion is well suited to a two-stage workflow where it serves as a spatial reasoning aid rather than final render geometry:

  1. Build your three-dimensional structure — a box, a wedge, a stepped form — using the extrusion tools in one geometry file. Use perspective rectangles and extrude downward or outward to establish correct proportions and foreshortening. Do not worry about face seams at this stage; this file is never assigned to a renderer.
  2. Create a separate geometry file for the final shapes. In the View section of its inspector, load the extrusion file as a geometry reference overlay and set a low opacity (0.3–0.4). The extruded structure appears as a faint blue wireframe aligned in the same normalised coordinate space.
  3. Trace each visible face as a single closed P2P polygon drawn directly over the reference. A front face becomes one polygon; the top surface becomes another; a visible side panel becomes a third. Each polygon is a clean, seamless shape with no internal edges.
  4. Assign different renderers or fill colours to each traced polygon to represent the distinct light-facing planes of the solid — lighter for the top, mid-tone for the front, darker for the side.

The extrusion file remains as a non-rendering scaffold. If you later need to adjust the structure — changing the angle of the perspective face, for example — you edit the scaffold geometry, reload it as the reference in the P2P file, and retrace only the affected shapes.

Morph Layer Import — building multi-state targets from subdivision

Loom's morph system interpolates between polygon vertex positions. For this to work, every morph state must have exactly the same number of polygons with exactly the same vertex count in each. The cleanest way to guarantee this is to produce all states from the same subdivision setup — varying only the PTW/PTP transforms — and assemble them into a single layered geometry file using Import layer….

How vertex count consistency is guaranteed

Baking a polygon with a fixed algorithm, subdivision level, and line ratios always produces the same vertex count regardless of what PTW or PTP transforms are active, because transforms move existing vertices without adding or removing them. Two bakes of the same source polygon at the same subdivision settings are therefore guaranteed to match, even if their point positions differ dramatically.

Step-by-step: assembling morph targets from subdivision bakes

  1. Set up the base subdivision state. In the Subdivision tab, configure the algorithm, levels, and line ratios you want. Disable all PTW and PTP transforms. This is your base state — the geometry as it naturally subdivides with no deformation.
  2. Bake the base state. Click the bake button. Give the output file a descriptive name such as myshape_base. The baked XML file is written to your project's polygonSets/ folder.
  3. Bake each variant state. Enable PTW or PTP transforms to create a deformed version of the same geometry — for example, spike outward, pinch inward, rotate children. Bake each variant under a different filename: myshape_expanded, myshape_pinched, etc. Keep the algorithm, level, and line ratios identical to step 1; change only the transform settings.
  4. Open (or create) the geometry document. In the Geometry tab, open or create the polygon set document that will hold all states as layers. Switch to the Editor.
  5. Import the base layer. In the Layers panel, click Import layer…. Navigate to the baked base file (myshape_base.xml) and click Import. It is added as a new layer named after the file.
  6. Import each variant layer. Click Import layer… again for each additional baked file. Each becomes its own named layer in the same document.
  7. Check the vertex-count warning. If an orange warning appears at the bottom of the layer panel, one or more layers have a different vertex count from the first layer. This means the bakes used different settings — correct the mismatch before proceeding. If counts match, no warning appears.
  8. Rename the layers. Select each layer and click Rename to give it a meaningful name: Base, Expanded, Pinched, and so on. Clear names make the document easier to work with when assigning morph targets in the Sprites inspector.
  9. Save the document. Click Save in the geometry editor toolbar. All layers are saved together in the single .json file. The file is now ready to use as a morph target source.
  10. Assign in the Sprites inspector. With the sprite selected, set Morph Target to this geometry document (or a specific named layer within it). Enable the Morph Amount driver and animate between 0 (base) and 1 (target). See Morph Targets for the full animation workflow.
Tip: You are not limited to one morph target. Loom selects a target layer by name. If you create multiple variants and assign each to a different sprite (or swap the target name at keyframe time), you can chain multiple morph states across a single animation without separate geometry files for each.
Note: Import layer… accepts both baked XML files (from the Subdivision tab bake button) and editable JSON geometry files (from the Geometry editor Save). When importing a JSON file, all visible polygons from all visible layers are merged into the new layer.

Pressure sensitivity

Loom can store pressure samples on freehand and traced geometry. Pressure data is used by SVG export and brush rendering to create hand-drawn variation in stroke weight and opacity. Geometry that contains pressure data is visually marked in the editor so it is clear which shapes carry pressure information.

Creating pressure data

  • Freehand drawing records pressure automatically when the input device provides it. If no device pressure is available, Loom still records a usable default profile.
  • Pressure Trace lets you post-add pressure to geometry created by points or point-by-point tools. Select a shape or edge, activate Pressure Trace, then trace along the relevant path.
  • Pressure tracing samples densely along the path so point-by-point geometry can acquire convincing variation rather than a simple straight interpolation.
  • Pressure can be discarded from selected geometry and re-traced if the result is not right.

Closed pressure-sensitive shapes

Freehand strokes can be finalised as closed polygons while retaining their pressure profile. Pressure remains attached to the path rather than being discarded when the shape becomes a closed polygon. This makes it possible to draw closed, pressure-sensitive regions and then feed them into subdivision and rendering.

Pressure and subdivision

When a pressure-sensitive polygon is subdivided, children can receive pressure in different ways from the Subdivision tab. See Subdivision Pressure for the current modes.

SVG Geometry Import

The Import SVG… button converts an SVG file's path data into a Loom polygon set. All styling — colour, stroke width, fill, opacity — is discarded; only the underlying Bézier geometry is imported. The result is a fully editable polygon set that can be subdivided, animated, and rendered exactly like hand-drawn geometry.

The primary use case is importing text as geometry: design lettering in Inkscape, convert glyphs to paths, and bring them into Loom for experimental subdivision and animation. The same workflow works for any vector art whose geometry you want to control inside Loom.

Inkscape workflow

  1. Design or import your text or vector art in Inkscape.
  2. Select all objects. Choose Object › Object to Path (or Shift+Ctrl+C on Windows/Linux). This converts text glyphs and shapes to explicit Bézier path elements — Loom cannot read live text nodes.
  3. Optionally group or arrange elements. Loom flattens all <path> elements from the entire file into one polygon set, so grouping affects only the SVG, not the import result.
  4. Save as Plain SVG (File › Save a Copy › Plain SVG) for the cleanest output. Inkscape SVG works too but contains extra metadata that is safely ignored.
  5. In Loom's Geometry tab, click Import SVG… and select the file. A new polygon set appears in the geometry list, selected and ready to use.
  6. Assign the polygon set to a sprite via Quick Setup or by editing the sprite's inspector.
Tip: After importing, open the geometry editor to inspect the result. Each polygon is one subpath from the SVG. For a word like "loom", you will see four closed polygons (plus inner contours for letters like 'o').

Element-level transform attributes — translate, scale, rotate, matrix, skewX, skewY — are resolved and applied to coordinates before import, including nested transforms inside <g> groups.

Coordinate mapping

Coordinates are normalised so that the larger SVG dimension (width or height of the viewBox) maps to the Loom geometry range [−0.5, 0.5]. Aspect ratio is always preserved. A sprite assigned this polygon set with the default scale of 1.0 will fill the canvas's shorter dimension; scale it down in the Sprites inspector to adjust.

If the SVG has no viewBox attribute, Loom falls back to the width and height attributes. If neither is present, a 100 × 100 unit canvas is assumed. For best results, ensure your SVG is exported with a viewBox (Inkscape Plain SVG always includes one).

Multi-contour glyphs

Letters with interior voids — o, a, e, B, D, P, Q, R and so on — have two or more contours (an outer shape and one or more inner holes). Each contour becomes a separate polygon in the imported set. When rendered with a filled renderer, the inner contours create the expected void appearance only if your renderer uses even-odd fill. With a stroked renderer all contours draw independently, which is usually what you want for subdivision experiments.

Tip: After importing a multi-letter word, try applying a subdivision set that uses stochastic or noise rules. Each letter's polygon set of contours subdivides independently, giving a naturally varied result across the word.

Deform Tool

The Deform tool applies radial falloff deformation to all geometry points within a configurable influence zone. It is designed for creating organic shape changes — swinging a tail, bulging a body, nudging a limb — without selecting and transforming individual points.

Unlike the Transform section, which acts on explicitly selected points, the Deform tool works by proximity: every point within the radius of influence is moved proportionally, with those closest to the centre affected most strongly. The tool is primarily interactive: you drag handles directly on the canvas to see the deformation live and commit each drag as a discrete undo step.

The first time you drag the operation handle (or press Apply), Loom automatically snapshots the layer state as the deform origin. The Reset to Base button in the inspector restores the layer to this snapshot at any time, with a full undo entry. This lets you freely experiment with multiple drag passes and return to the clean starting state if the result isn't working.

The Apply button remains available for numerical entry: type a precise angle, scale, or push value in the relevant field, then click Apply to commit it as an undo step.

Reference layer overlays

The Before and After pickers in the Deform inspector let you designate any existing layer as a ghost reference overlay while you are working:

  • Before — shows the chosen layer as a blue outline. Useful for comparing your current edit against the original base geometry.
  • After — shows the chosen layer as an orange outline. Useful for targeting a known destination state while deforming toward it.

Both overlays are drawn at reduced opacity and do not affect rendering or export. They are only visible while the Deform tool is active. Set either picker to None to hide the overlay.

Named point sets

For complex geometry — tentacles, wings, multi-limb rigs — the radial zone and sector arc may not precisely isolate the points you need. Named point sets let you save any selection of control points under a name and recall it instantly from a dropdown, so the same carefully chosen subset is ready the moment you need it across a session and across layers.

Creating a set:

  1. Switch to the Points tool and select the exact control points you want in the set.
  2. Switch back to the Deform tool.
  3. In the Point Set row of the inspector, click the + button. A small popover appears asking for a name.
  4. Type a descriptive name (e.g. Tip Points, Base Ring) and press Save or hit Return. The set is saved into the geometry file and the dropdown switches to it immediately.

Using a set: choose it from the Point Set dropdown. The chosen set's members are highlighted with gold rings on canvas so you can immediately confirm which points will move. Only those points are affected by subsequent deformation — the radial zone and sector still define how much each included point moves, but points outside the set are completely skipped regardless of their position.

Set Point Set back to None to return to normal (all-points) behaviour.

Managing sets: while a set is active, two extra buttons appear next to the dropdown:

  • Pencil — rename the active set.
  • Trash — delete it permanently (undo-able).

Cross-layer use (morph lock): point sets are stored by structural position — polygon index and point index within that polygon — rather than by per-layer UUID. This means a set defined in Layer A automatically selects the corresponding points in Layer B, Layer C, and so on, as long as all layers share the same topology (the same number of polygons with the same number of points each). Morph-target layers created by duplication always satisfy this condition. Switch the active layer, select the same set from the dropdown, and deform — the structurally identical points in the new layer are targeted.

Tip: You can combine a named point set with the Sector and Invert constraints. For example, on a tentacle rig you might define a Sucker Row point set, enable Sector to restrict influence to the bottom arc, and use Invert so the tip suckers bend more than the base ones — all in one deform pass.

Walkthrough: deforming a fish tail

This step-by-step example shows how to use the Deform tool's interactive handles to create a morph target representing the tail of a fish swung to one side — one extreme of a looping swim cycle.

Assumptions: you have a fish geometry set on a layer named Base containing a body polygon and a tail region. The fish is roughly centred on the canvas with the tail pointing to the right.

  1. Duplicate the base layer. In the Layers panel, duplicate Base and rename the copy Tail Left. Make Tail Left the active layer. This is the morph target you will shape; the original Base layer remains untouched.
  2. Activate the Deform tool. Click the wand.and.rays icon in the geometry editor toolbar.
  3. Set the Base layer as the Before reference. In the Deform inspector, set the Before picker to Base. A blue ghost outline of the original fish appears on canvas. This gives you a visual anchor so you can see how far the tail has moved as you work.
  4. Place the influence centre at the tail root. Click the canvas at the point where the tail joins the body — the hinge of the swing. A dashed circle and three handles appear.
  5. Resize the radius to cover the tail. Drag the white square handle (right edge of the circle) outward until the dashed circle encompasses all the tail geometry but leaves most of the body outside. A radius of roughly 0.20–0.30 is typical. The body points just inside the edge of the zone will flex slightly — this is intentional and creates a natural join.
  6. Enable the Sector constraint to exclude the body. In the inspector, turn Sector on. Two white-outlined circle handles appear on the perimeter of the influence zone. Drag them so that the clear (included) arc covers only the tail side of the centre, and the dark overlay covers the body side. For a fish with the tail to the right: drag both handles to sit roughly at the top and bottom of the circle, so the included arc sweeps the right half only. The dark shading on the left shows that body points there will be unaffected even though they are inside the radius.
  7. Set the operation to Rotate and enable Invert. Set Operation to Rotate, Falloff to Smooth, and turn Invert on. With the sector masking out the body and Invert ensuring the tip swings more than the root, both constraints work together to give a clean, natural tail flex. The top handle turns yellow.
  8. Drag the yellow operation handle to swing the tail. Drag the yellow circle at the top of the influence zone clockwise (downward and to the right). Only the tail-side geometry moves; the body stays still. The Angle ° field shows the current value. When the shape looks like a natural downswing (around −20° to −30°), release. The deformation commits as one undo step.
  9. Refine if needed. If the result looks too rigid at the body join, press ⌘Z to undo, then try a slightly larger radius and drag again. If the geometry looks good but the swing is too extreme, undo and use a smaller drag. Each attempt is a single undo step so iteration is fast.
  10. Add a Push pass to curve the fin tip. Drag the white square handle inward to tighten the radius so only the outermost fin points are covered. Switch Operation to Push in the inspector — the top handle turns orange. Drag the orange handle downward and slightly rightward (in the direction the tail is curling). This nudges the very tip of the tail in a trailing arc, making the movement feel more fluid. Release to commit.
  11. Create the opposite extreme. Duplicate Base again, name it Tail Right, make it the active layer, and repeat steps 4–9 dragging the yellow handle counter-clockwise (upswing, around +20° to +30°). You now have three geometry states: Base, Tail Left, and Tail Right.
  12. Wire into a morph cycle. In the Sprites inspector, assign the three layers as morph targets with appropriate in-weights and out-weights to create a smooth swim oscillation. The Deform tool has done the geometry work; the morph system handles the blending at render time.
Tip: For a more lively tail, run a second Rotate pass at a tighter radius centred further along the tail — away from the body join. This adds a secondary flex at the fin tip independent of the main swing, mimicking how a real fish tail bends more at the extremity than at the root. Each pass is its own undo step so you can back out either independently.
Note: The Deform tool affects all point types — anchors and both control handles. This preserves Bézier curve continuity across the deformation so subdivision output stays smooth even on heavily deformed shapes.

Sprites are the fundamental render units in Loom. Each sprite references a geometry file and is assigned a subdivision set and a renderer set. The Sprites tab shows a hierarchical tree of all sprites organized into named sets.

Sprite list

Sprites are grouped into sprite sets. Click the chevron to collapse or expand a set. Drag sprites within the list to reorder them, or drag between sets to reassign. Use the search bar at the top to filter by sprite or set name.

Each sprite row shows:

  • An enable checkbox — disabled sprites are not drawn.
  • The sprite name (double-click to rename).
  • A play icon if any animation driver is active.
  • For child sprites, P / R / S buttons to toggle parent transform inheritance.

Creating sprites

  1. Click + in the set toolbar to add a new sprite set, or use the sprite + button to add a sprite to the selected set.
  2. With the new sprite selected, open the inspector and assign a Geometry (polygon set file), a Renderer Set, and a Subdivision Set.
  3. Set initial Position, Scale, and Rotation in the Transform section.

Coordinate System

All sprite transforms — position, scale, and rotation — are expressed in Loom's world coordinate system. Understanding this system is essential for placing sprites precisely and for interpreting the values produced by animation drivers.

Scale X / Y Unitless multiplier. 1.0 = base size. 2.0 doubles the sprite; 0.5 halves it. Negative values mirror the geometry. X and Y can differ to stretch non-uniformly.

Depth & Parallax

Each sprite has a Depth value in its Transform section. Combined with the Perspective setting in the Global tab Camera section, depth creates a 2.5D parallax effect: sprites at different depths move at different rates when the camera pans or zooms, giving a sense of three-dimensional space without changing the underlying 2D geometry.

The depth formula

For each sprite the engine computes a parallax factor from its depth and the global perspective strength:

f = 1 / (1 + depth × perspectiveStrength)

This factor scales both the camera's pan offset and zoom for that sprite, every frame. At depth 0 (the focal plane) f = 1.00 and the sprite follows the camera exactly. Positive depth (background) gives f < 1 — the sprite lags and appears to zoom more slowly. Negative depth (foreground) gives f > 1 — the sprite leads and appears to zoom more aggressively.

Only the product depth × perspectiveStrength determines the parallax factor. A depth of 2 at strength 0.3 is identical to a depth of 200 at strength 0.003 — both give f = 0.63. Use whichever scale feels more natural for your scene. Small integer depths (1–5) with a strength around 0.3 are a good default.

When depth × perspectiveStrength approaches −1, the factor approaches infinity and a foreground sprite will fly to an extreme screen position. With strength 0.3, the singularity is at depth −3.3 — keep foreground depths above −3. With strength 0.5, the limit is around −2, so keep foreground depths above −1.5. With strength 0.1 there is plenty of room down to −9. Use fractional depths (e.g. −0.5, 0.5, 1.5) for finer-grained layer separation within the safe range.

Draw order

Sprites are automatically drawn back-to-front based on depth — the greatest depth value is drawn first (furthest away), the most negative depth is drawn last (on top). You do not need to manually order sprites to get correct occlusion between depth layers.

Quick setup

  1. In the Global tab, set Camera → Enabled on and enter a Perspective value — start with 0.3.
  2. In the Sprites tab, select each sprite and set its Depth in the Transform section. Use 0 for the focal-plane layer, small positive values (2–3 for background, 5 for far background) and small negative values (e.g. −0.5) for foreground sprites. Use fractional values for finer layer separation.
  3. Animate the camera Pan X/Y in the Timeline. Background sprites drift more slowly than focal-plane sprites, creating the parallax sense of depth.
Tip: Camera zoom animation also creates depth — zooming in makes background sprites grow more slowly than focal-plane sprites, giving a push-in feel. Both the pan offset and the zoom factor are scaled by f, so depth interacts equally with all camera motion.

Image Sprites

Any sprite can display an image file in place of its polygon geometry. Supported formats are SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, GIF, and WebP. macOS renders SVGs at full vector quality (no resolution loss at any scale); raster formats (PNG, JPG, etc.) render at their native pixel resolution and will soften if scaled up significantly. All standard transform drivers and camera features apply exactly as they do to polygon sprites.

This is the direct, intuitive path for incorporating bitmap artwork — photographs, painted backgrounds, conventional sprites, UI elements — without passing through the polygon or renderer pipeline at all.

Setup

  1. Place one or more image files in the project's svgs/sprites/ folder. Loom creates this folder automatically. Any mix of SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, GIF, or WebP files is accepted.
  2. In the Sprites tab, select the sprite.
  3. In the inspector's Sprite section, an Image dropdown appears (hidden when svgs/sprites/ is empty). Choose the filename.
  4. The sprite now renders the image. The renderer set and subdivision set are bypassed — the image carries its own visual styling.
  5. Adjust position, scale, rotation, and depth in the Transform section as usual. Animate any of these with the Animation drivers.
Tip: To revert to polygon rendering, set the Image picker back to None. The sprite's geometry and renderer set are preserved and resume immediately.

Wireframe positioning proxy

Image sprites have no polygon geometry, so they are invisible in the wireframe view and cannot be positioned visually using the standard drag tools. The solution is to assign a lightweight proxy polygon to the sprite's base shape fields. The wireframe draws this proxy; the renderer ignores it at runtime because the image draw path returns before any polygon rendering occurs.

  1. One-time setup — create the proxy shape. In the Geometry tab, create a polygon set. A 4-sided regular polygon works well; name the set something like _proxy and the shape square. You only need to do this once per project.
  2. Assign the proxy to the sprite. Select the image sprite in the Sprites tab. In the inspector's Shape section, set Shape Set to _proxy and Shape to square. The proxy square immediately appears in the wireframe view at the sprite's current position.
  3. Size the proxy to match the image. Use the sprite's Scale X and Scale Y fields to match the proxy's wireframe footprint to the intended on-screen area of the image. The image is rendered at the same transform, so the proxy is a true WYSIWYG stand-in.
  4. Position using the wireframe. Use the wireframe view to drag or numerically position the sprite as you would any polygon sprite. Camera panning, zoom, and parallax depth behave identically.
  5. Verify in the canvas. Switch to the live canvas to confirm the image appears where the proxy indicated. No further adjustment is normally needed.
All image sprites in a project can share the same proxy shape set. Each sprite's individual Scale and Position fields give it unique on-screen placement. You never need more than one proxy shape.
Why the proxy is invisible at runtime. When a sprite has an image assigned, Loom's render loop immediately routes to the image draw path and returns, bypassing the polygon pipeline entirely. There is no "turn off rendering" step required.

File location and loading

SVG files must be placed in <project>/svgs/sprites/. This folder is created automatically when a project is first opened. Loom loads all .svg files from this directory at project-open time. If you add or remove files while the project is open, use Reload (⌘R) to pick up the changes.

Performance

macOS caches a rasterised bitmap of each NSImage after the first draw at a given size. For a sprite whose scale does not change frame-to-frame — a static background logo, for example — the rendering cost after the first frame is negligible. For sprites with animated scale, macOS re-rasterises when the drawn size changes significantly, which adds a small per-frame cost proportional to the SVG complexity. Simple Inkscape exports (a few dozen paths) have no measurable impact. Very complex SVGs with thousands of paths (detailed illustrations, large type specimens) may add a few milliseconds per frame.

SVG sprites bypass Loom's polygon rendering pipeline entirely. They cannot be subdivided, and Loom's renderer sets (stroke colour, brush, stamp modes) do not apply. If you want Loom's rendering on SVG-derived geometry, use Import SVG… in the Geometry tab instead — this converts the path data to a polygon set that goes through the full subdivision and rendering pipeline.

Per-Variant Images

Each entry in a sprite's Shape Variants list can optionally carry its own image filename. When the shape driver selects that variant index, the image is rendered instead of the variant's polygon geometry — letting you mix Loom polygon variants and image variants freely on the same sprite.

Use case

A character sprite may have a polygon idle state (index 0) and a bitmap jump frame that was painted externally (variant 1). A single shape driver keyframe at the jump frame switches both the geometry slot and the image in one step. No separate sprite is needed for the image state.

Setup

  1. Place the image file in the project's svgs/sprites/ folder (same folder as regular image sprites — see Image Sprites for supported formats).
  2. In the Sprites tab, select the sprite. Open the inspector's Shape Variants section.
  3. Each variant row now shows two dropdowns: the existing sprite name picker on the left, and a new image file picker on the right. The image picker lists all files in svgs/sprites/; leave it at — to keep geometry rendering for that variant.
  4. Choose an image filename for any variants that should display an image.
  5. Enable the Shape driver on the sprite and add a keyframe selecting the image variant's index. At runtime the image renders at the sprite's full transform.

Behaviour rules

  • A variant with no image assigned (picker shows —) renders its geometry and renderer set as normal — no change to existing behaviour.
  • A variant with an image assigned renders the image instead, bypassing the polygon and renderer pipeline for that index — exactly as svgFilename does for the whole sprite.
  • Geometry variants and image variants can be freely mixed: index 0 can be a polygon shape while index 1 is a bitmap, or any other combination.
  • All transform drivers (position, scale, rotation, opacity, depth, camera) apply normally to the rendered image — the same set that applies to regular image sprites.
  • If the named file is not found in svgs/sprites/ at render time the variant falls back to its polygon geometry silently.
  • The base sprite (shape driver index 0) continues to use the top-level Image field in the inspector; per-variant images apply only to variant indices 1 and above.
Combining with the Cycle driver. Per-variant images work independently of the cycle driver. If both are active on the same sprite, the cycle driver takes priority for the cycle path; the shape driver handles variant image switching on a completely separate index track. Both compose naturally.

Pivot Offset

By default, rotation is applied around a sprite's position — the point you set in the Position row. For many animation tasks that is the right centre: a spinning coin, a wobbling plant. But for rigged characters, each limb needs to rotate around its joint, not its geometric centre. A lower leg should pivot at the knee, an upper arm at the shoulder. The Pivot field makes this possible without changing where the sprite is positioned in the world.

Pivot X / Pivot Y appear in the Transform section of the sprite inspector, between Rotation and Depth. The values are in the same world-coordinate units as Position. Rotation (from animation drivers, keyframes, or cycle pose overrides) is applied around position + pivot rather than position.

Setting a pivot

The pivot can be placed interactively on the canvas or entered numerically in the inspector.

Canvas drag (recommended). When a sprite is selected in the Sprites tab wireframe view, an orange crosshair-circle marker appears at its current pivot position (default: the sprite's centre when the pivot is at zero). Drag the marker to any point on the canvas to reposition the pivot; the view updates live. Release to commit. The marker is always drawn on top of the wireframe so it remains visible regardless of the sprite's geometry.

  1. Select the sprite in the Sprites tab wireframe view.
  2. Locate the orange crosshair marker on the canvas.
  3. Click and drag it to the joint — knee, shoulder, hip, or any rotation point.
  4. Test by giving the sprite a non-zero Rotation value. It should now arc around the joint.

Numeric entry. The Pivot row in the inspector's Transform section also accepts direct values in world units. The canvas marker and the inspector fields stay in sync.

Pivot X / Y are in world units, not pixels. The same unit system as Position: 100 units = half the canvas width. A pivot of (0, 15) moves the rotation centre 15 world units upward, which is 15% of canvas half-height. For a 1080×1080 canvas, 100 units = 540 px, so 15 units ≈ 81 px.

Pivot and hierarchy. For a rigged character with parent–child sprites, set the pivot on each child individually. The child's rotation is always applied around child.position + child.pivot in local space, before the parent's transform is composed on top. This means pivots work correctly at any depth in the hierarchy.

Reset Transform clears Pivot X and Y back to (0, 0) along with Position, Scale, and Rotation.

Rotation arc constraint

A pivot can optionally be locked to a fixed angular range — like a physical joint stop preventing a knee from bending backwards or an elbow from hyper-extending. Enable Rot range in the Transform section (a checkbox below the Pivot row) to activate the constraint for the selected sprite.

When enabled, two fields appear: Arc min° and Arc max°. The sprite's total rotation (base rotation plus any animation offset) is clamped to the range [min, max] at render time, before parent hierarchy composition. Animation drivers can freely produce values outside this range — they will simply be clamped, so the sprite appears to hit a hard stop.

Editing the arc on the canvas

When a sprite with an active constraint is selected in the Sprites tab wireframe view, a filled arc sector is drawn around the pivot marker showing the allowed rotation range. Two small circles mark the arc endpoints (minimum and maximum angles).

  1. Select the sprite in the Sprites tab wireframe view.
  2. Locate the orange pivot crosshair and the arc sector around it.
  3. Drag either arc-endpoint circle to set the minimum or maximum angle. The angle is measured from the positive X axis (3 o'clock) in standard mathematical convention (counter-clockwise positive).
  4. The inspector fields update live as you drag. Release to commit.
Min < Max is always enforced. If you drag a min endpoint past the max (or vice versa), Loom swaps them on commit so the constraint range stays valid.
Workflow for a simple two-joint limb. Place the upper leg sprite with Pivot Y at the hip offset, and the lower leg as a child of the upper leg with Pivot Y at the knee offset. Rotating the upper leg at the hip rotates the whole limb; rotating the lower leg at the knee bends it independently. Add pose overrides in your SpriteCycle (see Pose Overrides) to define each step of the walk.

Scenes

A project can contain multiple scenes. Scenes let one project hold several distinct animations — an opening sequence, a main loop, a title card — while sharing the same pool of geometry, subdivision rules, renderer sets, layers, and SpriteCycles. Each scene carries its own independent timeline: duration, timeline markers, and all sprite animation data (positions, keyframes, drivers).

The Scenes panel lives at the bottom of the left column in the Sprites tab, below the sprite list and toolbar. It is collapsible — click the chevron header to hide it when you need more vertical space for the sprite list.

What a scene contains

Every scene stores its own copy of:

  • Timeline duration — frame count and any named timeline markers.
  • All sprite animation — positions, scales, rotations, and every driver lane (keyframe, noise, oscillator, jitter) for every sprite in every set.
  • Canvas and global settings — frame rate, background colour, border, camera view angle, and related options that are typically set once but can differ per scene if needed.

The following are shared across all scenes and edited once for the whole project:

  • Geometry files, subdivision sets, and renderer sets.
  • Sprite definitions (names, enabled state, geometry/renderer assignments, hierarchy).
  • SpriteCycles.
  • Compositing layers (parallax, blur, blend mode, opacity).
  • Lighting configuration.
Practical implication: if you add a new sprite in the Sprites tab or change a renderer in the Rendering tab, that change applies to every scene. If you adjust a sprite's animation driver in the Timeline, that change applies only to the currently active scene.

Creating and switching scenes

The Scenes panel shows the list of scenes for the open project. The active scene is highlighted and its name is shown in bold.

To add a scene:

  1. Click the + button in the Scenes panel header.
  2. Type a name for the new scene in the alert (e.g. Main Loop, Intro, Outro). The name is pre-filled with the next available default name.
  3. Click Add. The new scene starts as a copy of the currently active scene's timeline and sprite animation, giving you a useful starting point to modify.

To switch scenes: click any scene row in the Scenes panel. The timeline, all sprite positions, and all animation drivers immediately update to reflect that scene. Rendering and playback always operate on the active scene.

Managing scenes

Rename: double-click a scene name in the Scenes panel. An alert appears pre-filled with the current name — edit it and press Rename.

Delete: click the − button on a scene row. The button is disabled when only one scene exists (a project must always have at least one). Deletion is undoable.

Reorder: drag a scene row up or down in the Scenes panel list. Scene order affects nothing about playback or rendering — it is purely for your own organisation.

Workflow suggestion. For a multi-part animation — introduction, main action, credits — keep all parts in one project so they share geometry and rendering assets. Create one scene per part. Render each scene independently using the current scene's duration, then composite the resulting image sequences in your video editor. Switching scenes is instant and the project file stays compact.

Cycles Tab

A SpriteCycle is a named, reusable sequence of geometry states that drives a sprite's shape and renderer selection frame by frame. Cycles are the tool for walk-cycle animation, image replacement, and cross-fade blends — situations where a sprite needs to step through a discrete series of shapes with optional transitions between them.

Cycles are project-level assets. They live in the Cycles tab, are edited in a dedicated pop-up editor, and are assigned to individual sprites in the Sprites inspector. Multiple sprites can share the same cycle.

Cycles vs shape sequences. The legacy Shape Sequence field on a sprite steps through polygon sets at a fixed frame rate. SpriteCycles offer more: independent hold and transition durations per state, cross-fade opacity blending, renderer-set overrides per state, four loop modes, and a dedicated visual editor. When a cycle is assigned to a sprite, it takes priority over any shape sequence on that sprite.

States & Transitions

Each cycle contains an ordered list of states. A state defines:

FieldDescription
Shape SetThe polygon set to use while this state is active. Must be registered in the project's Geometry tab. Leave blank for pose-override rig cycles — see note below.
ShapeThe specific shape (layer) within that polygon set. Leave blank for pose-override rig cycles — see note below.
RendererOptional renderer-set override. When set to Inherit, the sprite's own renderer set is used.

Trans framesHow many frames to cross-fade into the next state. 0 = hard cut (the default). During a transition Loom renders both states simultaneously — the outgoing state fading out, the incoming state fading in — on separate offscreen contexts composited at complementary alphas.

The total cycle length shown in the Cycles tab is the sum of all states' hold + transition frames, adjusted for the active loop mode.

Hard cuts and offscreen compositing. Hard-cut states (Trans frames = 0) are rendered directly into the main context with zero additional cost. Cross-fade transitions use an offscreen CGContext per layer and alpha-composite the result, so they carry a per-frame memory and GPU cost proportional to canvas size. Use transitions deliberately rather than by default.
Shape Set / Shape must be empty for pose-override rig cycles. When a cycle state has a Shape Set and Shape filled in, every sprite assigned to that cycle loads that polygon as its cycle-state geometry — including the container sprite and every child limb. At render time each sprite draws the full shared mesh independently, each displaced by its own depth in the parent-child hierarchy, producing multiple overlapping copies of the figure at different screen positions.

For rigged walk cycles and other pose-override animations, leave Shape Set and Shape blank in every cycle state. Each sprite then falls back to its own base geometry (its individual shape set, set in the Sprites inspector), and the cycle only controls the pose — rotation, scale, and position — via pose overrides. The two approaches are mutually exclusive: use geometry states (Shape Set/Shape filled) for single-sprite image-swap or polygon-swap cycles; use pose overrides (Shape Set/Shape empty) for multi-sprite rigged characters.

Pose Overrides

Each cycle state can carry a set of pose overrides — absolute world-space transforms (position, rotation, scale) for any number of sprites in the project. When a pose override is defined for a sprite in the active cycle state, it replaces that sprite's base def values (set in the Sprites inspector) for the duration of the state. Animation drivers (keyframes, noise, oscillators) still apply on top of the overridden values as usual, so secondary motion layers over a posed rig just work.

This is the mechanism for building rigged walk cycles in the Cycle editor: each state in the cycle defines a pose — where every limb is — rather than just swapping a geometry file. Combined with pivot offsets (see Pivot Offset), limb rotations happen around the correct joints.

Keep Shape Set and Shape empty when using pose overrides. Pose-override cycles and geometry-state cycles are mutually exclusive modes. If a cycle state has a Shape Set filled in, every sprite in the rig renders that shared polygon instead of its own limb geometry, producing duplicate figures at displaced positions. See the warning above in States & Transitions for the full explanation.

How interpolation works during transitions

When a cycle is in a cross-fade transition between two states at progress t (0 = fully outgoing, 1 = fully incoming), Loom computes a blended pose for each sprite:

  • Both states have a pose override for this sprite — position and scale lerp component-wise; rotation uses short-path interpolation (the delta is wrapped to ±180° before applying, preventing a limb from spinning the long way around a full revolution).
  • Only the outgoing state has an override — the sprite interpolates from the overridden values back toward its base def values.
  • Only the incoming state has an override — the sprite interpolates from its base def values toward the overridden values.
  • Neither state has an override — the sprite is unaffected; it uses its base def values throughout.

The blended pose is applied to both the outgoing and incoming geometry layer instances before they are rendered and composited. This means geometry cross-fades (shape/renderer transitions) and pose interpolation compose cleanly: the limb moves smoothly through the transition period while the geometry simultaneously blends.

Pose overrides vs. state registration offsets

Each cycle state also has Offset X / Y, Rotation, and Scale X / Y fields that are additive on top of the sprite's base transform. These are a quick way to nudge a single sprite's position or angle for one state without defining a full pose. Pose overrides are absolute replacements and cover any number of sprites, making them the right tool for full-rig poses. Both can be used simultaneously: the absolute pose override is applied first, then the additive state offset is applied on top.

Pose editor

Each expanded cycle state contains a POSES section for authoring pose overrides directly in the UI. The section appears between the renderer picker and the hold-frames controls when a state is expanded.

  • Add a pose override — click the + button next to the POSES header. A menu lists every sprite in the project that does not yet have an override in this state. Select a sprite to add it. The override initialises from the sprite's current base-def values (position, rotation, scale), so it has no visual effect until you change the values.
  • Edit values — each override shows three rows: Pos (X and Y world-space position), Rot (degrees), and Scale (X and Y multiplier). All fields use FloatEntryField: type a value and press Return or click away to commit.
  • Remove a pose override — click the × button next to the sprite name. The sprite reverts to its base-def values for this state.
Typical walk-cycle workflow. Create one state per step in the walk (contact, down, pass, up, repeat). For each state, add pose overrides for each limb sprite and rotate them around their respective joint pivots — knee, hip, shoulder, elbow. The transitions between states interpolate the poses smoothly.

Image states

Each cycle state has a Source toggle at the top of its expanded detail panel: Loom (the default) or Image. In Image mode the state references an image file — SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, or GIF — instead of a Loom polygon and renderer set. The hold frames, transition frames, easing, and loop mode all work identically regardless of source type.

Why image states?

Loom's renderer applies a single style uniformly to all polygons in a sprite. Image files carry their own per-element colour, texture, and shading, making them the natural tool for conventional animation sprites: a painted walk cycle, a photograph sequence, or a multi-coloured character where face, hair, and clothing each need their own colour. SVG images scale without resolution loss; PNG/JPG bitmaps give you full painted or photographic quality at a fixed resolution.

Setting up an image state

  1. Open the cycle editor and expand a state row.
  2. In the Source picker at the top of the detail section, select Image. The shape set, shape, and renderer controls are replaced by an Image File row.
  3. Click Choose… to open a file picker. Select any SVG, PNG, JPG, TIFF, or GIF file on disk. Loom copies it into the project's svgs/sprites/ folder automatically and stores the filename.
  4. The cycle editor preview updates immediately to show the image. The Loom canvas will show it after the project is reloaded (the engine loads svgs/sprites/ at project open time).

Rendering and transitions

Image states are drawn at the sprite's current transform — position, scale, and rotation keyframes all apply normally. Cross-fade transitions between image states, or between an image state and a Loom polygon state, composite correctly at their respective alphas via the same offscreen mechanism used for Loom transitions.

Project reload required for live canvas. The engine loads svgs/sprites/ once at project open. After adding a new file via Choose…, the cycle editor preview shows it immediately (it loads direct from disk), but the rendered canvas will not show it until you close and reopen the project.

Wireframe positioning proxy for cycle sprites

A cycle sprite — whether its states are Loom polygons, images, or a mix — has no base geometry displayed in the wireframe view unless the sprite's own Shape Set and Shape fields are populated independently of the cycle. The proxy strategy gives the wireframe something to show:

  1. One-time setup. In the Geometry tab, create a shape set (e.g. _proxy) containing a simple polygon — a 4-sided regular polygon works well. This is a project-wide resource; you only create it once.
  2. Assign the proxy to the sprite. In the Sprites tab, select the cycle sprite. In the inspector's Shape section, set Shape Set to _proxy and Shape to square. The proxy square appears in the wireframe at the sprite's world position.
  3. Size to match. Adjust Scale X and Scale Y until the proxy's wireframe footprint matches the intended on-screen area of the cycle. All rendering — Loom polygon states, image states — uses the same sprite transform, so the proxy is a true WYSIWYG stand-in.
  4. Position using the wireframe. Drag or numerically set the sprite's position as normal. Depth and camera responses apply identically.
All cycle sprites in a project can share the same _proxy shape set. Each sprite's individual Scale and Position fields give it unique on-screen placement.

Layers Tab

The Layers system lets you stack groups of sprite sets into independent rendering planes, each with its own parallax factor, blur radius, opacity, and blend mode. Layers are the primary tool for parallax scrolling, depth-of-field effects, and compositing-level colour treatments.

When no layers are defined (the default), all sprites render into a single shared context in depth order — the legacy behaviour. As soon as at least one layer exists, sprites assigned to a layer are rendered in that layer's offscreen context and composited onto the main canvas in layer order (bottom to top).

Unassigned sprites. Sprites not assigned to any layer are rendered first into the main context using the legacy flat depth-sort. They appear beneath all layers. This preserves backward compatibility: existing projects with no layers load and render identically to before.

Layer list

The Layers tab shows a bottom-to-top stack. Layers are rendered in list order — the first layer in the list is drawn first (furthest back). Use the + and − toolbar buttons to add and remove layers. The eye icon toggles layer visibility. Each row shows the layer name, assigned sprite set names, a parallax badge (when not 1×), and a blend mode badge (when not Normal).

Reordering layers

Drag any layer row to a new position in the stack. To drag: hover over a row until the grip handle appears on the left edge, then click and drag it up or down. The layer snaps into place when you release. Render order updates immediately — the first row in the list is always drawn first (bottom of the compositing stack).

Reordering layers changes compositing order but does not reassign sprite sets. Sprites in each layer stay where they are; only the order in which the layer buffers are composited onto the canvas changes.

Parallax

The parallax factor multiplies the camera's pan offset before applying it to a layer. It affects only camera translation (pan) — zoom and rotation are not scaled.

The calculation is straightforward: layerOffset = cameraOffset × parallaxFactor. Because the camera offset is added directly to the screen position of every sprite in the layer, the factor controls how far sprites in the layer shift when the camera pans:

FactorBehaviourUse for
0Layer does not move — completely fixed to the canvas regardless of camera panDistant sky, static backdrop
0.1–0.4Very slow drift — layer appears far awayFar background elements
0.5–0.8Moderate drift — layer feels at mid distanceMidground scenery
1.0Moves exactly with the camera — same as unassigned spritesStandard foreground / subject layer
> 1.0Moves faster than the camera — layer appears very closeHyper-foreground overlay elements
< 0Counter-motion — layer moves opposite to the camera pan directionAbstract or stylised effects; not naturalistic
Classic parallax lives in 0–1, not 0–2. A common mistake is assigning a background layer a high factor (e.g. 2) and a foreground layer a low one (e.g. 0). This inverts the visual depth order — the "background" races past faster than the foreground, which looks wrong. For natural depth parallax, set the background to a low value near 0, and let the foreground sit at 1. Reserve values above 1 for elements that are intentionally closer than the main subject.

To build a parallax scene:

  1. Create three or more layers — Background, Midground, Foreground.
  2. Assign sprite sets to each layer.
  3. Set Parallax to 0–0.2 for Background, 0.5 for Midground, 1.0 for Foreground. This gives the strongest classic parallax contrast.
  4. In the Global tab, enable the Camera and add Pan X/Y keyframes. All layers will pan at their respective speeds.

Blur & Depth of Field

Setting a non-zero Blur radius on a layer applies a Gaussian blur to the entire composited layer after all its sprites have been rendered, but before the layer is composited onto the canvas. This makes it possible to simulate depth-of-field: sharp subjects on one layer, blurred foreground and background on adjacent layers.

A common DOF setup:

  • Background layer — Blur 12–20 px, parallax 0–0.2. Distant, soft, barely moving.
  • Subject layer — Blur 0 (sharp), parallax 1.0. This is the in-focus plane.
  • Foreground layer — Blur 8–15 px, parallax 1.2–1.5. Close, soft, faster-moving.

The blur driver lets you animate the blur radius — useful for rack-focus effects or for transitioning between focus planes over time.

Performance. A non-zero blur radius triggers an offscreen CGContext render for the layer, followed by a CIGaussianBlur Core Image pass, followed by a compositing draw. The cost scales with canvas size and blur radius. Keep blur radii as small as they need to be, and avoid blurring layers that contain many brush or stamp sprites (high polygon counts compound the offscreen cost).

Redraw Modes

By default every layer is cleared and redrawn from scratch on every frame — exactly like a normal animation cel. The Redraw section in the layer inspector offers two additional modes for layers that do not need to be regenerated every frame, or that should intentionally accumulate their history.

OnceDrawn once on the first frame; never redrawn.Static background plates: a painted sky, a watercolour wash, a bitmap texture. Costs one render call at startup and zero thereafter.

Accumulate — the Retain slider

When a layer is set to Accumulate, a Retain slider (range 0.50–0.99) appears below the mode picker. It controls what fraction of the previous frame's content is kept each frame before the new frame is painted on top.

  • Higher value (e.g. 0.97–0.99): slow fade — ghost trails persist for many frames, giving a long-exposure or smeared-ink look.
  • Lower value (e.g. 0.50–0.80): fast fade — each frame's content dissolves within a handful of frames, leaving only a faint motion shadow.

The fade is toward the project's background colour (set in the Rendering tab), not toward transparency. At 30 fps, the approximate half-life (time for old content to reach 50% opacity) is:

half-life (frames) ≈ log(0.5) / log(Retain)

For example, Retain 0.95 → ~13-frame half-life (~0.4 s at 30 fps). Retain 0.99 → ~69-frame half-life (~2.3 s).

Scrubbing and seek behaviour

Redraw mode affects how layers behave when you scrub the timeline or jump to a specific frame:

  • Full: no persistent buffer — no issue. Every frame is independent.
  • Once: buffer is preserved on seek. The static plate is frame-independent and does not need to be regenerated.
  • Accumulate: buffer is cleared on seek. The trail must restart from the new playhead position rather than carry forward a trail from a different part of the timeline. This is the correct behaviour — it matches how physical multiple-exposure effects work. After seeking, accumulation builds up again as playback proceeds.

Three-layer composition pattern

The motivating use case for per-layer redraw modes is a composition like this:

Layer (bottom→top)ModeRole

MidgroundAccumulate (Retain 0.95)Softly accumulating movement — figures, particles, or brushwork that leave a dissolving trail.
ForegroundFullSharp, fully-redrawn animated layer on top.

Each layer is composited independently with its own parallax factor, opacity, and blend mode, so the three effects coexist without interfering.

Transform Tab

The Transform tab is where Loom's generative power lives. It manages transformation parameter sets — ordered sequences of algorithms that progressively fracture and grow geometry into more complex polygonal structures. Each set contains up to five independent pipeline stages run in order: Involution (subdivision algorithms), Curve Refinement (curve-space post-processing), Segment Extraction (selective segment harvesting), Extension (branching from open curves or extruding polygon edges), and additional stages as the Geometric Lifecycle pipeline develops.

Working with sets

  1. Click + in the Sets toolbar to create a new subdivision set and give it a name.
  2. Click + in the Parameters toolbar to add the first algorithm to the set.
  3. Select the parameter row and adjust its settings in the inspector on the right: algorithm type, ratios, inset, visibility rules, pressure handling, and optional PTW/PTP geometric transforms.
  4. Click the eye icon next to the set to preview the subdivided result on the canvas — the sprites assigned to this set update live.
  5. When satisfied, click Apply in the Apply Bar to commit the set assignment to the selected sprite, or use the bake button to write the subdivided geometry to a permanent file.

Set and parameter controls

  • The bottom toolbar is grouped into set controls, parameter controls, and bake/export controls.
  • Subdivision parameters can be renamed with the angled pencil icon. Concise names make long recursive chains easier to read.
  • PTW and PTP sections stay collapsed until opened. PTP remains disabled until explicitly enabled, preventing accidental point-level deformation.
  • The SVG button exports a wireframe vector file without baking; the bake button writes static subdivided geometry back into polygonSets/.

Edge Curvature — CP Ratios and CP Normals

Internal connector edges are the lines that algorithms draw inward — from split points on the outer polygon boundary to the polygon centroid (Quad, Tri) or between computed midpoints (TriStar, Split). By default they are geometrically straight. Two complementary parameters control their shape:

CP ratios — parametric distribution

CP ratios place the two Bézier control points at specific positions along the connector line (0 = start anchor, 1 = end anchor). Because all four Bézier points are collinear, the curve remains geometrically straight regardless of the ratio values. What the ratios do control is the parametric speed along the edge: they determine where subdivision algorithms place new vertices when they later split this edge, and therefore subtly reshape the tessellation topology at deeper levels. The default values of 0.25 / 0.75 give a symmetric, uniformly distributed result.

CP normals — perpendicular offset

CP normals displace each control point perpendicular to the connector segment by an amount expressed as a fraction of the segment length. This lifts the control points off the straight line and produces genuine Bézier curvature. X offsets CP1 (near the start); Y offsets CP2 (near the end).

Normalise checkbox

The perpendicular direction is inherently ambiguous: it has two sides. The Normalise checkbox resolves this:

  • Off (default) — positive normals always go to the left-perpendicular of the edge direction (i.e. the geometric "left" as you travel from start to end). The bow direction depends on how each edge is wound in the polygon, so adjacent edges may curve in opposite or inconsistent directions. Useful for exploratory results and asymmetric effects.
  • On — positive normals always point away from the polygon centroid, negative normals toward it. This gives consistent outward or inward bowing on every internal edge regardless of winding. Use this when you want a regular "puffed" or "pinched" look across all children.
Tip: Start with equal X and Y values (e.g. 0.3 / 0.3) and Normalise on. Increase toward 1.0 for dramatic bowing; go negative for inward pinch. Once you have the overall shape, try X and Y values that differ to create asymmetric curves — a gentle approach at CP1 and a sharper exit at CP2, for example.
Note: CP normals apply only to internal connector edges — the lines drawn by the algorithm from split points or midpoints to the centroid. The outer edges of the source polygon are split using the Bézier scalaSplit method and are not affected by these settings.

Custom Compositor

The Custom Compositor lets you define your own subdivision algorithm from scratch. Instead of choosing one of the built-in types, you describe what happens to a single edge — which points to place near it, and what child polygons to build from those points. The engine then repeats your definition for every edge of every polygon, automatically generalising it to triangles, quads, and any higher-order polygon without further effort from you.

To open it, set the Algorithm picker to Custom for any subdivision generation. A Custom… button appears; click it to open the compositor as a sheet.

The per-edge template model

Every point and every child polygon you define is relative to the current edge. The engine iterates over all edges of the input polygon and applies your template at each one. This means you never need to count sides or hard-code coordinates — your algorithm works identically on a triangle, a quad, or an arbitrary polygon.

Cross-edge references

Any user point named X is also automatically available as prev.X and next.X. These evaluate the same point definition against the previous or next edge respectively. Edge Children

Edge Children define the actual output polygons. Each child is one polygon that the engine generates for every edge iteration. You must define at least one Edge Child with a point sequence of at least two points before any output appears in the preview.

  1. Click + Child in the Edge Children section header.
  2. Expand the child row and optionally name it.
  3. Use the Add dropdown at the right of the point chip row to append point references one at a time. The chips appear in sequence order.
  4. Build up the sequence to form a closed polygon. The engine connects each pair of consecutive points with a Bézier connector and closes back to the first point.
The order of points in the sequence determines polygon winding. Consistent winding matters for rendering fill correctly. Go either clockwise or counter-clockwise and be consistent across all your children.

Worked example: Quad-style subdivision

To reproduce a Quad-like split (four children sharing a common centroid point):

  1. Add a user point named mid, Kind = Edge fraction, t = 0.5.
  2. Add one Edge Child. Build its sequence: V.start → mid → C → prev.mid.
  3. The preview shows 4 quadrilateral children arranged around the centroid, one per edge. Set Preview level to 2 or 3 to verify the pattern recurses correctly.

Breaking down the sequence: V.start is the current edge's start vertex; mid is this edge's midpoint; C is the polygon centre; prev.mid is the midpoint of the previous edge. Together they trace one quadrant without gaps or overlaps.

Global Child (optional)

Some patterns require a single polygon connecting one point from each edge — for example, an inner ring that links all the edge midpoints together. Toggle Global Child on and choose a point name. The engine collects that one named point from every edge iteration in order and builds a single closed polygon from the resulting sequence.

This is how algorithms like TriStar produce their interior fill polygon. It is independent of any Edge Children you have defined and is generated once per input polygon rather than once per edge.

Preview

The dark preview panel on the right of the compositor sheet renders the algorithm live against a test square. The Preview level control (1 / 2 / 3 in the toolbar) sets how many rounds of subdivision to apply. Use level 2 or 3 to verify that recursion generalises correctly — that child polygons are themselves subdivided cleanly without gaps, overlaps, or degenerate geometry.

If the preview is blank, check that you have at least one Edge Child with a point sequence of at least two points. A message reading "No output — add an Edge Child and define its point sequence" confirms that the algorithm produces no polygons yet.

Saving

Click Save in the compositor toolbar. The algorithm is written back to the subdivision generation's params and immediately used by the live render in the main canvas. Custom algorithms are stored inline in project_config.json alongside all other subdivision parameters and round-trip cleanly through save/load.

Tip: Custom algorithms respect all the same subdivision parameters as built-in types — CP ratios, CP normals, visibility rules, PTW, and PTP all apply. Use CP normals in the main inspector to add curvature to the connectors between your anchor points.

Curve Refinement

Curve Refinement passes run after the Involution subdivision stage and operate in curve space — they adjust the curvature of Bézier control points without changing the polygon topology. This is the right place for global-curvature shaping, smoothing, and curvature-inversion effects that would be awkward to express as subdivision rules.

Each refinement pass has a Name, Enabled toggle, and an Algorithm picker. Multiple passes chain in list order, each receiving the output of the previous.

Step-by-step: adding curve refinement to a transform set

  2. In the inspector, scroll to the Curve Refinement section and click + to add a pass.
  3. Choose an algorithm from the Algorithm picker. Start with Smooth for general curvature softening.
  4. Adjust the pass parameters. Observe the preview canvas updating in real time.
  5. Add further passes as needed. Drag to reorder. Toggle Enabled off to temporarily bypass a pass without deleting it.

Segment Extraction

Segment Extraction passes run after Curve Refinement. Instead of generating new geometry, they filter the existing polygon set — selecting a subset of segments by index, length, or angle criteria and emitting only those as standalone open-curve or closed-polygon outputs. This is the right tool when subdivision produces a large symmetric mesh and you want to use only its diagonals, every nth edge, or the longest edges to build a sparser structure.

Step-by-step: extracting every other segment from a subdivided polygon

  1. Create or select a transform set that already has subdivision passes producing the mesh you want to sample.
  2. In the Segment Extraction section, click + to add a pass.
  3. Set Mode to Every Nth and set N to 2. This emits segments at indices 0, 2, 4, …
  4. Set Output type to Open curve if you want stroke-rendered lines, or Polygon if you want filled shapes.
  5. Adjust Offset (0–N−1) to shift which segment in each N-group is extracted without changing N.
  6. Check the preview canvas to confirm the expected pattern.
Input geometry: Segment Extraction operates on the polygon set produced by the stages above it (Involution + Curve Refinement). It does not consume the original base geometry directly — the segment indices it refers to belong to the subdivided output polygons.

Extension — Branch (open curves)

The Branch operation grows a recursive tree from the two endpoints of any open spline polygon in the scene. At each endpoint the algorithm places a scaled, rotated copy of the original open curve; each copy is itself treated as a new node and branched again to the specified depth. The result is a parametric fractal tree structure derived entirely from the input curve's shape.

Input requirement: Branch only acts on polygons with type openSpline. Closed splines and other polygon types are passed through unchanged. Make sure your geometry source is an open curve set, not a polygon set.

Branch geometry overview

Given an open curve with a start endpoint and an end endpoint, at depth 1 the engine places one copy of the original curve at each endpoint, rotated so that the copy's start aligns with the endpoint tangent and offset by Branch angle degrees. At depth 2 each of those copies is itself treated as a root and branched again. The scale of each successive level is multiplied by Scale ratio, so the tree naturally tapers toward the tips.

Step-by-step: creating a branching tree from a single open curve

  1. In the Geometry tab, draw an open curve using the Freehand or Point-by-point → Finalise open curve workflow. A single straight line with one Bézier segment works well as a first test.
  2. Save the open curve to a curve set (click Save in the geometry editor toolbar). It will be stored in curveSets/.
  3. In the Sprites inspector, run Quick Setup with the curve set as the source. Assign a stroke renderer (e.g. a thin line brush).
  4. Open the Transform tab. Select (or create) the transform set assigned to this sprite.
  5. In the Extension section, click + to add a pass. Set Type to Branch.
  6. Set Depth to 2 and Count to 2 to start. You will see 4 + 16 = 20 branches added to the canvas.
  7. Set Branch angle to 45°. This opens the tree symmetrically. Adjust upward (60–90°) for a wide bush, downward (15–30°) for a tall narrow tree.
  8. Set Scale ratio to 0.65. Each successive level will be 65% the size of its parent.
  9. Adjust Angle jitter (0–15°) for organic variation. Leave at 0 for a perfectly symmetrical fractal.
  10. Once satisfied, increase Depth to 3 or 4 — but read the caution below first.

Animating branches

Branch angle supports a DoubleDriver. Wire it to a noise or oscillator driver to make the tree breathe — branches fan out and close over time. Because the tree is recomputed every frame, any driver change propagates instantly through all levels. Keep driver amplitude below 30° for readable motion; large swings at high depth can flip branches through each other.

Extension — Extrude (closed polygons)

The Extrude operation grows closed polygon faces outward from selected edges. For each target segment of a closed spline polygon it constructs a new 4-segment closed polygon: the inner face (the original segment), two straight wall segments, and a bowed outer face. Multiple Generations stack by extruding the outer face of each previous result, building layered wall structures.

Input requirement: Extrude only acts on polygons with type spline (closed splines). Open curves are passed through unchanged. Use the Geometry editor to confirm your source polygons are closed before expecting extrusion output.

Step-by-step: extruding a subdivided polygon's edges

  1. Create a transform set with a subdivision pass that produces the closed polygons you want to extrude (e.g. Quad at level 2 produces 16 quads).
  2. In the Extension section, click + and set Type to Extrude.
  3. Set Target to All Edges. Each quad will grow outward from all four sides. For a sparser look, try Longest Edge to extrude only the longest side of each polygon.
  4. Set Distance to roughly 0.05–0.15 (in normalised canvas units). This is the height of the extrusion wall. Start at 0.1.
  5. Leave Width at 1.0 (parallel walls). Values below 1.0 taper toward a point; above 1.0 flare outward. Try 0.8 for a trapezoidal cross-section.
  6. Set Curvature to 0.0 for straight outer edges, or a small positive value (0.1–0.3) to bow the outer face outward. Negative values bow inward (concave).
  7. Set Generations to 1 for a single wall. Increase to 2 or 3 to stack concentric extrusion layers. Each generation extrudes the outer face of the previous result at the same distance and width.

Combining Branch and Extrude: Branch and Extrude are separate Extension passes and can both be enabled in the same transform set. They run independently on their respective polygon types — Branch on open curves, Extrude on closed polygons. A common pattern: draw an open curve tree (Branch pass) alongside a subdivided polygon mesh (Extrude pass) in the same sprite so that the branching organic structure and the extruded rigid structure share the same transform and renderer.

Evolution — Momentum Drift

Momentum Drift displaces a chosen subdivision parameter over time using a slowly-changing noise field. Unlike a DoubleDriver, which applies an independent value each frame, drift has inertia: the direction of change accumulates, producing organic wandering rather than oscillation. The displacement is fully deterministic and seekable — the value at any frame is computed directly from the seed and frame index without simulating prior frames.

The drift is computed as a closed-form weighted sum: each frame's noise value is weighted by momentum^k where k is how many frames ago it occurred. High momentum means old frames still contribute strongly; the form wanders in long sweeps. Low momentum means only recent frames matter; the form jitters rapidly.

Where drift applies: Evolution passes modify subdivision parameters before SubdivisionEngine runs. The displaced value is not saved — it exists only during that frame's render. The base parameter value in the inspector is the resting position.

Step-by-step: adding momentum drift to a subdivided polygon

  1. Select a transform set that has at least one subdivision pass (e.g. Quad at level 2).
  2. In the Evolution section of the inspector, click + to add an evolution pass. The default operation type is Momentum Drift.
  3. Set Target to Line Ratio XY to drift the subdivision split point on both axes simultaneously — this is the most visually active target for most algorithms.
  4. Start with Momentum at 0.85. This gives smooth, medium-speed drift. Values above 0.95 produce very slow sweeping motion; values below 0.5 produce rapid nervous jitter.
  5. Set Strength to 0.08–0.12. This is the peak displacement added to the base line ratio. A strength of 0.1 shifts the ratio from its base value by up to ±0.1. Keep strength below 0.25 to avoid the subdivision collapsing to degenerate geometry.
  6. Set Frequency to 0.02 (one noise cycle per 50 frames at 24 fps). Lower values produce slower drift; higher values produce faster direction changes. Very high values (>0.1) start to look like random jitter again.
  7. Leave Seed at 0. Change it to get a completely different trajectory without touching any other parameter.
  8. Play back and observe. Adjust Momentum up to slow the sweep or down to increase its responsiveness. If the geometry looks chaotic, reduce Strength.

The result is compound organic motion — the split position wanders while the curve bow independently breathes — that is far richer than any single animated DoubleDriver can produce.

Evolution — Convergence Pressure

Convergence Pressure gradually lerps all subdivision parameters of the current transform set toward a named target set. As pressure increases, the geometry slowly takes on the character of the target. At full pressure (1.0) the geometry exactly matches the target set's parameters. Pressure can be driven — starting at zero and ramping up over time — so the form evolves from its original character into a new one.

Convergence pressure can compose with momentum drift: drift makes the form wander stochastically, while pressure focuses that wandering toward an attractor. At low pressure the form roams freely; as pressure rises, the roaming becomes increasingly constrained and directed.

What is lerped: Line ratios, CP normal offsets, and inset scale/rotation are lerped toward the target. The target set must have the same number of subdivision passes; passes beyond the target count are left unchanged. Algorithm type, PTW, and stochastic parameters are not lerped — only the primary numeric geometry parameters.

Step-by-step: converging between two transform set states

  1. Create a source transform set (e.g. "Quad Loose" — Quad with line ratios 0.3 / 0.7, giving an off-centre split).
  2. Create a target transform set (e.g. "Quad Centred" — Quad with line ratios 0.5 / 0.5, giving a symmetric split). The two sets should use the same algorithm and pass count.
  3. Select the source set. In the Evolution section, click + and set Type to Convergence Pressure.
  4. Set Target set to Quad Centred using the picker.
  5. Leave Mode at Hold. Set the Pressure driver to a Ramp from 0 to 1 over 120 frames. Assign the source set to your sprite. On playback, the geometry gradually morphs from the asymmetric split to the symmetric split over 5 seconds.
  6. To loop the convergence, set Mode to Loop and Duration to 120 frames. The pressure will automatically cycle 0→1→0→1 regardless of the driver value — set the driver to a constant 1.0 and let the mode control the envelope.
  7. For a single convergence that comes back, use Oscillate mode: the pressure follows a sin wave from 0 to 1 to 0 over the duration, then holds at 0.

Evolution — Generational

Generational evolution is a small artificial-life system: instead of perturbing subdivision parameters like Momentum Drift and Convergence Pressure do, it iteratively mutates the actual polygon geometry across a fixed number of generations. Each generation applies exactly one mutation operator — Extrude (push one or more contiguous edges outward, adding a new connected quad) or Split (insert a new point on a random edge and displace it outward, growing a spike) — to one eligible closed polygon in the set, chosen by weighted probability. Every generation builds on the previous one's output, so the shape's history compounds: a spike added in generation 3 can itself be extruded or split in generation 7.

Deterministic, not closed-form: unlike Momentum Drift and Convergence Pressure, generation N genuinely depends on generation N−1's materialized geometry, so there is no formula that jumps straight to an arbitrary generation — the chain is recomputed from generation 0 every time it's needed. It is still fully deterministic and free of any state that persists between rendered frames: the same seed and parameters always reproduce the identical generation history, computed fresh each time from scratch. Scrubbing the timeline backward, or looping a Reveal driver (below), "de-evolves" cleanly — there is no ratchet.

Step-by-step: growing a shape over generations

  1. Select a transform set applied to a closed-polygon sprite. In the Evolution section of the inspector, click + and choose Generational from the type menu.
  2. Under Generations, set Count to 8 and leave Seed at 0 — every generation's mutation is derived from this seed, so changing it produces a completely different evolutionary path.
  3. Leave Vertex budget at its default. This is a hard cap on total point count across the whole set; extrusion and splitting both add points every generation, so a generation that would exceed the budget is rejected outright rather than producing runaway complexity.
  4. Under Extrude, set Weight to 1.0 and leave Run length at 1–2 and Distance at 0.05–0.2. Under Split, set Weight to 0 to get an extrude-only evolution for this first pass — a shape that sprouts flap-like extensions.
  5. Play back or bake the set. Every frame shows the fully-evolved 8th generation, since the Reveal driver is off by default — see below to animate the growth instead of seeing it fully-formed immediately.
  6. Once the extrude-only version looks right, try Split Weight: 1.0, Extrude Weight: 0.3 for a mix dominated by spiky growth with occasional flaps, or equal weights for an even mix.

Step-by-step: animating the reveal (discrete or tweened)

By default the full generation count is applied statically on every frame — the shape doesn't change over time. To watch it evolve, enable the Reveal driver under the Generations section.

  1. With a Generational pass selected, expand Reveal and enable the driver.
  2. Set the driver's mode to Keyframe, with a keyframe at frame 0 valued 0, and a second keyframe at (for example) frame 240 valued 8 (matching Generations → Count). Playing back now reveals one generation at a time as the frame advances from 0 to 240.
  3. The reveal is tweened automatically: at frame 150 (partway between the two keyframes), the generation in progress at that moment shows its extrude/split mutation growing smoothly toward full size — a flap mid-extrusion, or a spike partway to its final displacement — rather than popping in fully-formed the instant its generation is reached. Earlier generations are already fully settled; later ones haven't started.
  4. For a discrete "one generation at a time" look instead of a smooth grow-in, use the same keyframe setup but keep each generation's Distance/Displacement range narrow (e.g. a fixed 0.15–0.15) — the tween still happens, but a small, fast-settling range makes each step read as more of a "pop" than a visible grow.
  5. For a form that evolves and resets indefinitely, set the driver's mode to Oscillator or use Loop keyframes instead of a one-shot ramp — the shape will grow to full generation count then restart from nothing.
  6. Scrub the timeline freely in either direction — because the whole chain recomputes from generation 0 each time, moving backward correctly un-grows the shape rather than getting stuck at its most-evolved state.

Step-by-step: a smooth up-and-down reveal with Oscillator

Oscillator mode grows the shape to full Count and back down, repeating, with no separate "reset" jump — good for a continuously breathing form rather than a grow-then-restart cycle.

  1. With Generations → Count set to, say, 26, expand Reveal, enable the driver, and set Mode to Oscillator.
  2. Set both Base and Amplitude to half of Count (13, for Count 26). The oscillator output is base ± amplitude, so this centres the swing exactly on [0, Count] — leaving Base at its default with only Amplitude set will produce an off-centre range and clip against 0 or Count, sitting flat there until the wave swings back into range.
  3. Set Wave to Sine (smooth easing at both ends) or Triangle (constant-speed ramp, sharp reversal at the peaks). Avoid Square — it holds flat at each extreme for half the cycle, which is exactly the "dead period" this setup is meant to avoid. Avoid Sawtooth — it doesn't reverse smoothly, it ramps up then snaps discontinuously back to the start.
  4. To make playback start exactly at generation 0 rather than mid-swing, set Phase to 0.75 — both Sine and Triangle sit at their minimum a three-quarters of the way through a cycle, and phase is measured in cycle units (1.0 = one full period). Leaving Phase at its default 0 starts the animation already at the midpoint generation, moving upward.
  5. Freq (Hz) controls how long one full up-and-down sweep takes: period in frames = project fps ÷ Freq. Pick a value that gives a pace you like — e.g. at 24fps, Freq 0.1 gives a 240-frame (10 second) sweep.

Step-by-step: a different shape each time it cycles

By default, an Oscillator or Loop-keyframe Reveal retraces the identical growth every cycle — same seed, same generation history, just replayed. Enable Vary seed per cycle to make each cycle mutate differently.

  1. With a looping Reveal driver already set up (Oscillator, or Keyframe with Loop/Ping-pong), enable Vary seed per cycle just below the Reveal driver.
  2. Play back across several cycles. Each time the reveal restarts from generation 0, it grows into a different shape — the base Seed field is combined with an internally-tracked cycle count to produce a different effective seed per cycle, without you needing to change Seed by hand or script anything.
  3. The variation is still fully deterministic: the same cycle always regrows the same shape (scrubbing within a cycle works exactly as before), only which cycle you're in changes what it grows into.
  4. This has no effect with Reveal off, or with a one-shot (non-looping) Keyframe track — both cases have no "cycle restart" moment to key the seed change off.
  5. To catch and keep a generation you like as it cycles past: watch the Evolution Seed readout at the bottom of the Global tab's inspector (it tracks whichever Generational pass is selected here). When you see a shape you want, copy the seed number shown there into this pass's Seed field and turn Vary seed per cycle back off — the pass now always regrows that exact shape instead of continuing to vary.

Dissolution — Entropy

Entropy causes a polygon to progressively lose its geometric specificity over time. The form does not disappear — it forgets its original character: corners round, the shape simplifies, and it approaches the target shape asymptotically. Entropy operates on the fully-built output polygon (after subdivision, extension, and evolution) each frame.

Because entropy uses a closed-form calculation (factor = 1 − (1−rate)^frames), any frame is computable independently �� it is fully seekable and deterministic.

  1. In the Transform tab inspector, select a transform set and expand the Dissolution stage section.
  2. Click + to add a dissolution pass. Select it to open the Dissolution inspector.
  3. Under Entropy, enable Enabled.
  4. Choose a Target: Smoothed rounds corners while retaining the polygon's overall gesture; Centroid collapses the form inward to a point; Circle normalises vertices to a best-fit circle radius.
  5. Set Rate. At 0.005, the form reaches roughly half-decay after 140 frames (about 6 seconds at 24 fps). At 0.02, half-decay in about 35 frames. Rates above 0.05 decay very fast.
  6. Leave Noise at 0 for uniform decay. Increase it (try 1���5 canvas units) to introduce per-vertex irregularity so the path toward simplicity feels organic rather than mechanical.
  7. Change Seed to get a different noise pattern without altering any other behaviour.

Dissolution — Collapse

Collapse causes the form to disappear at a specific trigger moment — either abruptly or over a brief shrinking window. Unlike entropy, there is no gradual degradation before the moment of collapse; the form is entirely itself, then gone. Collapse and entropy compose: a common pattern is entropy first (the form erodes), followed by collapse (it finally vanishes).

Collapse is fully deterministic and seekable. The trigger frame is computed once from the parameters and seed, the same way on every frame.

  1. In the Dissolution inspector, expand the Collapse section and enable Enabled.
  2. Choose a Trigger:
    • Frame count — set Frame to the number of frames before collapse. Simple and predictable.
    • Probability — set Probability to the per-frame chance of collapse. 0.01 gives an expected lifetime of 100 frames; 0.002 gives 500 frames. Exact timing varies per sprite (governed by the Seed), but the average is 1/P frames.
  3. Choose a Mode:
    • Instant — the form disappears in a single frame. Best for sharp, graphic effects.
    • Brief — the form shrinks toward its centroid over Duration frames before vanishing. 6–12 frames is a fast but visible implosion; 24+ frames makes the collapse feel deliberate.
  4. Choose After (end mode):
    • Remove — the form is gone permanently for the rest of playback.
    • Loop — after collapsing, the dissolution cycle restarts from frame zero. The form reappears at full complexity and begins eroding and collapsing again. With Entropy active, this produces a continuous cycle of growth → erosion → collapse → rebirth.
    • Respawn — same as Remove for now; integration with Fulguration (Phase 7) is pending.

Lights Tab

The Lights tab provides a simple theatrical lighting system. Lights produce a greyscale (or coloured) light map that is multiplied onto opted-in layers — lit areas show through at full brightness, unlit areas go to black. The system is intentionally non-physical and designed for expressive stage-lighting effects: vignettes, spotlights, area washes, and animated light.

Enabling lighting

Lighting has two switches:

  • Master switch — the lamp icon in the Lights toolbar. When off, no lighting pass occurs anywhere in the render, regardless of individual light or layer settings. Zero performance cost when disabled.
  • Layer switch — each layer has a Lighting toggle in the Layers inspector. Only layers with Lighting on receive the light map multiply.
By default all layers have Lighting off and the master switch is off. This means existing projects load with no change to rendering behaviour.

Layer scoping

By default every light affects all layers that have Lighting enabled. You can restrict a light to a specific subset of layers so that, for example, a nighttime ambient on the foreground does not flatten an interior scene on a background layer that has its own lamp.

Each light has a collapsible Layers section at the bottom of its inspector. It lists every project layer with a toggle switch:

  • When all layers are toggled on (the default), the field stores an empty list and the light affects every lit layer — identical to the previous behaviour.
  • Toggling a layer off removes it from the light's scope. The engine renders a separate light map per layer, containing only the lights assigned to it, so each layer receives its own independent lighting.
  • If you retoggle all layers back on, the list resets to empty (the "all layers" state), recovering the cached-map performance benefit.
Multi-layer scene example. A two-layer scene has a dark night exterior (foreground) and a warm lamp-lit interior (background). Add one Omni for the night ambient — scope it to the foreground layer only. Add a second small Omni at the lamp position — scope it to the background layer only. Each layer receives its own light map; neither light bleeds into the other's space.

Lighting recipes

All values use canvas-normalised units where 0.5 = half the canvas width. Intensity is 0–1. Falloff 2 (quadratic) is the default and suits most cases; raise it for a harder edge, lower it for a broader soft wash. Colours are described as tints — white preserves source colours exactly.

Start with intensity lower than you expect. The multiply blend is non-linear: an intensity of 0.8 already produces a visibly dark scene. Most expressive lighting sits between 0.05 and 0.6.

Performance

When the master switch is off, the lighting pass is completely skipped — zero overhead. When on, the engine generates one CoreGraphics light map per frame (using gradient drawing) and applies a multiply composite per lit layer. For static lights (constant drivers, no animation), the light map is cached and reused on every subsequent frame at near-zero cost. For animated lights the map is recomputed each frame; for typical theatre rigs (2–6 lights, 1–2 lit layers) this adds well under 5 ms per frame.

Audio Tab

The Audio tab is a timing reference tool, not a playback synchronisation engine. The animation does not run in lock-step with the audio; instead you use the audio to listen, orient yourself in the piece, drop markers at meaningful moments, and then place or adjust keyframes at those frame positions. This separation keeps the audio workflow lightweight — you can move through a sequence at your own pace and only cross-reference the animation when you are ready.

Audio is stored in a project subfolder (audio/) alongside your geometry and sprite files, so the whole project remains self-contained and portable.

Importing audio

Click the download icon in the Audio tab toolbar to open the file picker. Supported formats are WAV, AIFF, MP3, M4A, CAF, FLAC, and AAC — anything AVFoundation can decode on macOS. The selected file is copied into the project's audio/ subfolder (created automatically on first import) and the amplitude envelope is computed immediately in the background.

To swap the audio file, import again — the new file replaces the old one. To remove audio entirely, click the − button in the toolbar; this clears the waveform, stops playback, and removes the reference from the project (the copied file remains on disk in audio/).

Waveform display

The main panel shows a full-width amplitude envelope for the entire file. The envelope is pre-computed once at import time — Loom reads all samples, divides them into 2000 equal time buckets, takes the peak amplitude per bucket, and normalises so the loudest point fills the display. This gives a stable, consistent picture of the audio's shape regardless of the file's absolute level.

The display is fixed to the full file duration; there is no zoom or horizontal scroll. This is intentional — the envelope is a navigation aid for recognising where sounds fall in time, not an editing surface.

  • Playhead — white vertical line with a small triangle handle at the top. Moves in real time during playback.
  • Markers — orange vertical lines with short labels. Position corresponds to a frame number.
  • Time scale — tick marks along the bottom edge at regular intervals (interval chosen automatically from the file duration).
  • Click to seek — click anywhere on the waveform to move the playhead to that position.

Markers

Markers record a named frame position in the audio. Use them to tag dialogue cues, musical beats, sound effects, or any other moment you want to reference when placing keyframes.

Dropping a marker

Press ⌘M, or click the Drop Marker button in the left panel, to place a marker at the current playhead position. The marker's initial label is the timecode at the moment of dropping (e.g. 0:04). Markers are kept sorted by frame at all times.

Repositioning a marker

Hold Shift and drag on or near a marker's orange tick in the waveform view. The marker snaps to your pointer within a 12-point grab radius. While dragging, the marker turns yellow and displays a circle handle. A marker cannot be dragged past its immediate neighbour in either direction — there is always at least a one-frame gap between adjacent markers.

Renaming a marker

Double-click the marker label in the left-panel list to edit it. Press Return to confirm.

Notes

Click the pen icon in the Markers heading to show or hide a notes field on every marker row. Each notes field accepts up to three lines of free text — useful for a fragment of dialogue, a description of the corresponding action, or a reminder about which sprite or driver to key. Notes are hidden by default to keep the list compact.

Jumping to a marker

Click the orange pin icon on any marker row in the left panel to seek the audio playhead to that marker's time position.

Removing a marker

Click the × button on the right side of the marker row.

Timing workflow

A typical session using audio for timing:

  1. Import your audio file. Let the waveform compute (usually under a second for a short clip).
  2. Play through the audio once using the Play button. Listen for the moments that should drive key poses or actions — dialogue starts, beats, impacts.
  3. Play again and press ⌘M at each moment you want to mark. Don't worry about precision — you can shift-drag markers on the waveform afterwards to fine-tune them.
  4. Rename each marker to describe the action it corresponds to: word "and", foot lands, door opens.
  5. Note the frame numbers shown under each marker label. Switch to the Timeline and place keyframes at those frames.
  6. Refer back to the audio at any time by switching to the Audio tab and clicking a marker's pin to jump to its position.
The frame number shown for each marker is calculated from the audio position and the project's Target FPS (set in the Global tab). If you change the target frame rate, existing marker frame numbers will no longer correspond to the same audio position — re-drop or reposition them after any FPS change.

Progressive brush mode

In Progressive mode, brush strokes can be drawn gradually across frames. With global animation enabled, the path advances from first polygon/edge toward last; in static rendering it draws the complete path.

Colour, width, opacity, and blur drivers

Renderer properties can be static values or animated via drivers. Renderer drivers differ from sprite drivers: they affect only a specific renderer layer. This is especially useful when a renderer set uses Playback All.

Per-renderer Gaussian blur

Every renderer has a Blur radius field (in logical pixels, scaled by the quality multiplier at render time). When non-zero, the renderer's output is drawn into an offscreen buffer, a Gaussian blur is applied, and the blurred result is composited back onto the canvas. Renderers with blur = 0 use a zero-overhead direct path.

Blur radius can be animated with the Blur Driver — the same scalar driver types available for stroke width and opacity. The timeline adds a Blur lane (mint colour) to each renderer's driver lanes.

  4. Set Color A — this is the base colour the driver starts from (equivalent to the old constant value). Set Color B — the target colour at the other end of the blend.
  5. Adjust the mode-specific parameters: Range / Amplitude / Freq and Phase as needed.
  6. For Jitter and Noise, change Seed to differentiate sprites using the same renderer set — otherwise all sprites will produce identical colour values each frame.
Tip: For a subtle organic breathing effect, use Noise with Amplitude 0.15 and Period 60. For a hard strobe, use Oscillator with a square wave and Freq 2 Hz. For a gentle pulse, use Oscillator with a sine wave and Freq 0.5 Hz.
Jitter and Noise both use the sprite's index as part of their deterministic hash, so different sprites in the same scene will produce different random colour sequences even with the same Seed. The Seed parameter lets you offset those sequences further when you want correlated or uncorrelated groups.

Palette

The inspector offers a Palette section for managing named colour swatches. Palettes can be saved to a JSON file and imported into other projects. This allows colour schemes to be shared across projects without manually re-entering values.

Animation

Loom animates sprite, camera, and renderer properties via drivers — self-contained generators that produce a value for each frame. Sprite drivers affect the entire sprite; renderer drivers affect individual renderer layers inside a renderer set. Every driver has an enabled flag; drivers are off by default and must be explicitly enabled before they affect rendered output.

Scale timing

Keyframe timing scale lets you stretch or compress a group of keyframes in one step — useful when a section of animation is paced too fast or too slow and you would otherwise need to reposition many individual keyframes by hand.

Workflow

  1. Select the keyframes you want to retime. Use ⌘-drag for a rubber-band region, click a lane label for a full lane, or press ⌘A to select everything.
  2. When two or more keyframes are selected, a scale field appears in the top-left corner of the timeline header alongside the zoom buttons. It shows 100 (percent).
  3. Type a new percentage value:
    • > 100% — spreads keyframes further apart (slower timing, longer animation)
    • < 100% — compresses keyframes closer together (faster timing, shorter animation)
  4. Press Return or click the ↔ button to apply. The scale field resets to 100 and the selection is cleared.

How the pivot works

Scaling always pivots around the earliest selected keyframe. That keyframe stays fixed; all other selected keyframes move relative to it. This means scaling a group of keyframes that starts at frame 30 leaves frame 30 in place and stretches or compresses the subsequent keyframes away from it.

To retime the whole animation relative to frame 0, use ⌘A (select all) before scaling — the earliest keyframe in the entire project becomes the pivot, which is typically frame 0.

Scope and undo

Scale applies to all selected keyframe types together: sprite transform lanes (position, scale, rotation, morph, opacity, shape), renderer driver lanes (fill, stroke, width, opacity), and camera lanes. The operation is a single undoable step — press ⌘Z to reverse it in full.

Tip: Scaling can cause two keyframes in the same lane to land on the same frame when compressing aggressively. The values are preserved but the duplicate frames will be adjacent in the sorted lane — drag one to a different frame or delete it to clean up.

Visual sprite keyframe editing

When a Position, Scale, or Rotation keyframe is selected in the timeline, Loom lets you set its value by directly manipulating the sprite in the Edit or Parallax wireframe view — no need to type numeric values.

How it works

  1. In the timeline, click a diamond on a sprite's Position, Scale, or Rotation lane to select it. The playhead moves to that frame.
  2. The sprite is automatically selected in the wireframe view and a gold Timeline KF badge appears in the control strip, showing which lane is active (e.g. Timeline KF · Position).

Camera animation

The Timeline has a dedicated Camera section with four independently driven lanes. Camera animation is evaluated from the same global frame clock as sprite and renderer drivers, and is applied as a final viewport transform after all sprite, subdivision, and renderer animation.

Tracking vs Pan — a critical distinction

Both Tracking and Pan move the viewport, but they mean different things and diverge the moment zoom or rotation is involved.

When they produce the same result: at zoom = 1.0 and rotation = 0°, tracking(X, Y) and pan(−X, −Y) are numerically identical. In a flat animation with no zoom or rotation, the two drivers overlap completely.

When they diverge: the moment zoom ≠ 1 or rotation ≠ 0°, tracking continues to hold the world point at screen centre, while pan delivers the same raw pixel offset regardless of zoom level. If you key a pan of −200 px at zoom 1× and then animate zoom up to 2×, the pan stays −200 px and the world point drifts off-centre. With tracking(200, 0) and zoom 2×, the engine automatically computes an offset of −400 px, keeping the point centred.

Using them together: tracking and pan compose additively. A common pattern is to set tracking to follow a focal world point and add a small pan on top to create a cinematic drift or composed reframe — the tracked point is almost-centred but offset by the pan amount.

Depth & Parallax with camera animation

When sprites have non-zero Depth values and Perspective strength is non-zero, the engine re-evaluates the camera transform through each sprite's depth factor before rendering it. Morph targets

Morphing smoothly interpolates a sprite's geometry between two states: the base geometry and a morph target geometry. The Morph Amount driver (0 = base, 1 = target) controls the blend. Both geometries must have the same number of polygons with identical vertex counts — the interpolation pairs vertices by index.

Basic workflow

  1. Ensure both the base and target geometry have matching polygon and vertex counts.
  2. Assign the base geometry to the sprite as normal.
  3. In the inspector, set the Morph Target field to the target geometry document (or a specific named layer within it).
  4. Enable a Morph Amount animation driver — Oscillator for continuous looping, Keyframe for manually timed transitions.
  5. Use Morph Lock on the target geometry document to prevent accidental topology edits.

Building morph targets from subdivision states

The most reliable way to create matched morph geometries is to derive all states from the same subdivision bake setup, varying only the PTW/PTP transforms between bakes. This guarantees identical vertex counts because transforms move vertices without adding or removing them. Use Import layer… in the Geometry editor's layer panel to assemble multiple baked states into a single layered document.

See the full step-by-step guide at Morph Layer Import in the Geometry section.

Tip: Morph Amount is not limited to 0–1. Values above 1 exaggerate the morph beyond the target; negative values push geometry in the opposite direction. Both produce valid (if extreme) results when used intentionally.

Set swap drivers

Two sprite drivers allow the active subdivision parameter set and the active renderer set to be animated — switching which named set is applied to a sprite on any given frame. These drivers operate on set names (strings) rather than numeric values, so they use step semantics: the transition is immediate, not interpolated.

Subdivision Set driver

When enabled, the Subdivision Set driver overrides the sprite's static subdivision set assignment each frame. The result is that the subdivision structure of the sprite changes discretely over time — morphing from one algorithmic family to another, or cycling through a set of structural variants.

Because Loom recomputes subdivision every frame, this has no additional performance cost: the engine is already calling the subdivision algorithm for every frame; this driver simply changes which parameter set is passed to it.

Renderer Set driver

When enabled, the Renderer Set driver overrides the sprite's static renderer set assignment each frame. The sprite's rendering style — colour, stroke mode, brush, stamp — changes discretely on the frames you specify. This is useful for creating structural visual variation, cycling through different aesthetic treatments, or producing flicker and strobing effects in combination with jitter mode.

Driver modes

Both drivers share the same three modes:

No interpolation occurs — the switch is instantaneous. Loop, Once, and Ping-Pong loop modes are available. Easing algorithms

Easing controls the velocity profile of a keyframe segment — how fast the interpolated value moves through its range between two keyframes. Every keyframe has an individual easing setting that applies from that keyframe to the next. Easing affects all driver types equally: position, scale, rotation, morph, opacity, shape, and all renderer driver lanes.

All easings map a linear progress value t ∈ [0, 1] to an eased output in the same range. The output reaches exactly 0 at the start keyframe and exactly 1 at the end keyframe in all cases — only the shape of the journey between them differs.

The three families

The 16 available easings span four distinct speed profiles — flat (Linear), polynomial (Quad, Cubic), sinusoidal (Sin), exponential (Exp), and overshoot (Back) — each available in three directional variants:

VariantMeaningShape
In Slow start, fast finish. Acceleration from rest. Concave curve — stays near zero longer before accelerating.
Out Fast start, slow finish. Deceleration to rest. Convex curve — moves quickly then tapers to the end value.
In-Out Slow start and slow end, fastest in the middle. S-shaped curve — symmetrical, both extremes are gentle.

Linear

No easing applied. The value advances at a perfectly constant rate. Mechanical and precise — useful when you want explicitly robotic timing or when you are chaining many keyframes that collectively form a hand-crafted velocity curve.

Quadratic (²) and Cubic (³)

Polynomial easings are the workhorses of animation. Quadratic (t²) gives a moderate, smooth ease. Cubic (t³) is noticeably stronger — slower starts and faster midpoints, with a crisper arrival or departure. Use quadratic for everyday position and scale transitions; use cubic when the motion needs more personality or punch.

Sine (sin)

Sine easing uses a quarter or half period of a cosine/sine wave instead of a polynomial. It is softer than quadratic — the curve is less aggressive at the extremes, producing the most natural-feeling motion of all the families. Use sine for organic movement, breathing effects, slow floats, or any animation that should feel effortless rather than driven.

Exponential (exp)

Exponential easing uses a base-2 power curve (210t−10). The difference from cubic is dramatic: the value barely moves for most of the segment then accelerates explosively, or vice versa. Use expo for snap zooms, dramatic entrances, reveal effects — any moment where you want a decisive, almost discontinuous-looking change. The curve is nearly flat at the quiet end, so easing values near 0 or 1 are almost imperceptibly small.

Back (overshoot)

Back easing goes slightly past the target value before settling. On In back, the value pulls back in the opposite direction first (anticipation) before moving toward the destination. On Out back, it overshoots the end value and rebounds (follow-through). In-Out back combines both. The overshoot amount is fixed (approximately 10% past the value range) — it does not require configuration. Values momentarily outside [0, 1] are produced intentionally; this is physically correct and expected.

Full round-trip workflow

  1. Draw geometry in Loom's editor or design text in Inkscape.
  2. Export SVG from the Geometry tab to svgs/. Open in Inkscape and add colour, gradients, and fills.
  3. Save the styled version to svgs/sprites/. In Loom, assign it as an SVG sprite — this gives you the Inkscape visual style with all of Loom's transform and camera animation.
  4. Optionally, also Import SVG… from the Geometry tab to bring the stripped path geometry back as a polygon set — animate the outline separately with Loom's subdivision and renderers running on the same shape.

Video export

  1. Click the movieclapper icon in the toolbar to open the Export Sheet.
  2. Review or adjust the Frame Range (Start and End frame). These default to the project's playback range — see Frame Range below.
  3. Set the desired FPS (24, 25, 30, or 60). Total frames and Duration in seconds update immediately.
  4. Choose a Codec: H.264, HEVC (H.265), or ProRes 4444.
  5. Toggle Restart from beginning if the playhead should reset before export starts.
  6. Click Export…, choose a destination file, and wait for the progress indicator to complete.
The exported video resolution is canvas size × Quality multiplier. Higher quality values increase render time and file size but do not change the frame range or frame rate. ProRes 4444 produces larger files but preserves alpha and is better for compositing workflows.

Frame range

The frame range controls which portion of the animation is captured, for both live playback and video export.

FieldLocationBehaviour
StartToolbar & Export SheetFirst frame to include. 0 = from the very beginning. The engine advances silently through any pre-start frames before capturing begins.
EndToolbar & Export SheetLast frame (exclusive). When not set, Loom derives this from the longest keyframe lane. For purely parametric animations the toolbar shows ∞.
Total framesExport Sheet (derived)End − Start. Read-only; updates as you change Start, End, or FPS.
DurationExport Sheet (derived)Total frames ÷ FPS, shown in seconds. Read-only; use this to verify the exported clip length without doing the arithmetic yourself.

Keyframe-driven animations

When the project contains keyframes, the End field in the toolbar shows the frame of the last keyframe across all driver lanes. You can shorten the range by entering a smaller End value, or extend it to export past the last keyframe (useful when drivers use Loop Once and you want to capture the held final pose).

Purely parametric animations

Drivers that use Sine, Noise, Drift, or other formula modes without keyframes have no natural stopping point. The toolbar End field shows ∞ and playback continues until you press Stop.

To export a parametric animation, click ∞ in the toolbar to enter a concrete end frame, or open the Export Sheet — it initialises End to 10 seconds worth of frames at the project FPS, giving you an immediate starting point to adjust.

Mixed animations

Most projects combine keyframe and parametric drivers. The effective end frame is derived from the keyframe data; the parametric layers simply keep running past that point if they have no natural stopping point of their own.

Output folder

Click the folder icon in the toolbar to open the most recently used renders folder in Finder. Loom remembers the last export directory across sessions.
